## 1.1 BUILDING POWER: TENANT ASSOCIATION FORMATION

### Why Organize?

**Collective power is essential for:**
- Holding landlords accountable
- Accessing TCAP and cooperative ownership
- Building community resilience
- Protecting tenant rights
- Creating long-term housing stability

### Step-by-Step Organizing Guide

**STEP 1: IDENTIFY CORE ORGANIZERS (Weeks 1-2)**

**Activities:**
- Identify 3-5 trusted tenants willing to take leadership
- Assess building conditions and tenant concerns
- Conduct informal conversations with neighbors
- Map out which tenants might be allies

**Questions to Answer:**
- Who are the natural leaders in the building?
- What are the top 3 issues affecting tenants?
- Are there language or accessibility barriers to address?
- What is the building's organizing history?

**Tools Needed:**
- Tenant roster (if available)
- Building layout map
- Issue survey (informal)

---

**STEP 2: BUILD RELATIONSHIPS (Weeks 3-6)**

**Activities:**
- Door-knock every unit multiple times
- Host informal gatherings (potlucks, building events)
- Create communication channels (WhatsApp group, email list)
- Listen deeply to tenant concerns
- Identify shared grievances

**Organizing Principle:** **"People organize around their own issues, not yours."**

**Sample Door-Knock Script:**

> "Hi, I'm [Name] from Apartment [#]. I've been living here for [X] years. I'm talking to neighbors because I've noticed [issue - e.g., heat problems, leaks, roaches]. Have you experienced this too? I'm thinking we could work together to fix these problems. Would you be interested in coming to a building meeting next week?"

**Follow-Up:**
- Get contact information (phone, email, preferred language)
- Invite to first building meeting
- Follow up with text or call reminder

---

**STEP 3: HOLD FIRST BUILDING MEETING (Week 7)**

**Preparation:**
- Choose accessible location (community room, local church, park)
- Provide childcare if possible
- Prepare food (builds community!)
- Translate materials into building languages
- Invite supportive partner organizations

**Meeting Agenda (90 minutes):**

**Welcome & Introductions (15 min)**
- Go around room: Name, apartment, one hope for the building
- Establish ground rules (respect, confidentiality, one mic)

**Share Stories (30 min)**
- "What brought you here today?"
- Collective storytelling about building conditions
- Identify common themes

**Vision & Goals (20 min)**
- "What would a healthy, safe building look like?"
- Brainstorm priorities
- Dot-vote on top 3 issues

**Next Steps & Structure (20 min)**
- Discuss forming a tenant association
- Elect interim leadership or steering committee
- Set next meeting date
- Form working groups (communications, research, actions)

**Closing (5 min)**
- Collective commitment: "Will you come to the next meeting?"
- Celebratory moment (chant, song, clapping)

**Post-Meeting:**
- Send thank-you texts to all attendees
- Share meeting notes
- Follow up with people who couldn't attend

---

**STEP 4: FORMALIZE THE ASSOCIATION (Weeks 8-12)**

**Legal Structure Options:**

**Option A: Unincorporated Association (Simpler)**
- Adopt bylaws
- Elect officers (President, Vice President, Treasurer, Secretary)
- Open bank account (requires EIN)
- No legal incorporation necessary initially

**Option B: Incorporated Nonprofit (More Formal)**
- File Articles of Incorporation with NYS
- Obtain 501(c)(3) or 501(c)(4) status (if seeking tax exemption)
- More paperwork but greater legal protections

**Essential Documents:**

‚ñ° Bank account

### Your Rights as a Tenant

**BEFORE Cooperative Conversion:**

‚úÖ **Right to organize** without landlord retaliation  
‚úÖ **Right to habitable housing** (heat, hot water, no hazardous conditions)  
‚úÖ **Rent stabilization protections** (if applicable)  
‚úÖ **Right to Counsel** in eviction proceedings  
‚úÖ **Protection from harassment** (NYC Admin Code ¬ß 27-2004)  
‚úÖ **Notice of sale** (90 days before property is sold - TCAP provision)  
‚úÖ **Right of first opportunity** to purchase building  

**DURING Cooperative Conversion:**

‚úÖ **Voluntary participation** (you cannot be forced to become a co-op member)  
‚úÖ **No rent increases** due to conversion  
‚úÖ **No evictions** based on conversion participation  
‚úÖ **Right to remain as renter** with continued subsidy if you choose not to participate  
‚úÖ **Full disclosure** of financial information and risks  
‚úÖ **Access to legal counsel** through TCAP legal services  
‚úÖ **Time to make informed decisions** (no pressure tactics allowed)

**AFTER Cooperative Conversion:**

‚úÖ **Democratic control** (one member, one vote)  
‚úÖ **Ownership equity** in your home  
‚úÖ **Continued subsidy support** (if eligible)  
‚úÖ **Protection from displacement** (30-year affordability covenant)  
‚úÖ **Right to sell shares** (subject to resale restrictions)  
‚úÖ **Technical assistance** (years 1-2 minimum)  
‚úÖ **HPD oversight** to ensure financial stability

### Anti-Retaliation Protections

**It is ILLEGAL for a landlord to retaliate against you for:**

‚ùå Organizing a tenant association  
‚ùå Reporting code violations  
‚ùå Requesting repairs  
‚ùå Withholding rent due to uninhabitable conditions  
‚ùå Expressing interest in TCAP  
‚ùå Attending tenant meetings  
‚ùå Filing complaints with HPD or housing court

**Signs of Retaliation:**
- Sudden rent increases (if not rent stabilized)
- Threats of eviction
- Harassment or intimidation
- Denial of lease renewal
- Frivolous legal actions
- Utility shutoffs

**What to Do:**
1. **Document everything** (take photos, save texts/emails, keep written log)
2. **Report to HPD** (call 311 or file online complaint)
3. **Contact legal services** (Right to Counsel providers, TCAP legal aid)
4. **Inform your tenant association** (strength in numbers)
5. **File housing court claim** (if necessary - you have a private right of action)

## 1.3 MEETING FACILITATION GUIDE

### Principles of Effective Meetings

**Good meetings are:**
- **Accessible** (childcare, interpretation, wheelchair access)
- **Democratic** (everyone has voice, decisions are collective)
- **Action-oriented** (clear next steps and accountability)
- **Inclusive** (welcoming to new people and diverse perspectives)
- **Efficient** (respect people's time)

### Meeting Roles

**Facilitator:**
- Keeps meeting on track and on time
- Ensures everyone can participate
- Enforces ground rules
- Remains neutral on content

**Notetaker:**
- Records key points and decisions
- Tracks action items and who's responsible
- Shares notes after meeting

**Timekeeper:**
- Monitors time for each agenda item
- Gives time warnings
- Helps group stay on schedule

**Vibes Watcher:**
- Monitors group energy and dynamics
- Suggests breaks or shifts when needed
- Ensures inclusive atmosphere

### Sample Meeting Agenda (90 minutes)

**OPENING (10 minutes)**
- Welcome and introductions
- Review agenda (allow additions)
- Establish ground rules
- Celebrate wins/good news

**UPDATES (15 minutes)**
- Brief reports from committees
- News relevant to building
- Announcements

**EDUCATION/SKILL-BUILDING (20 minutes)**
- Workshop on TCAP process
- Guest speaker
- Know Your Rights training
- Skills practice

**DISCUSSION/DECISION-MAKING (30 minutes)**
- Present issue or proposal
- Discussion and questions
- Decision-making (consensus or vote)

**ACTION PLANNING (10 minutes)**
- What are next steps?
- Who will do what by when?
- Support needed?

**CLOSING (5 minutes)**
- Recap decisions and action items
- Confirm next meeting
- Appreciations

## 1.5 CONFLICT RESOLUTION FRAMEWORKS

### Common Sources of Conflict in Tenant Associations

- Leadership struggles and ego
- Language and cultural differences
- Different priorities and organizing styles
- Personal conflicts between neighbors
- Disputes over money and resources
- Differing levels of risk tolerance
- Mistrust or lack of communication

### Conflict Resolution Steps

**STEP 1: EARLY INTERVENTION**
- Address concerns quickly before they escalate
- Create space for private conversations
- Listen without judgment

**STEP 2: FACILITATED DIALOGUE**
- Neutral facilitator guides conversation
- Each person shares perspective uninterrupted
- Identify shared goals and values
- Brainstorm solutions together

**STEP 3: MEDIATION** (if needed)
- Bring in external mediator
- Structured process with ground rules
- Written agreement on resolution

**STEP 4: DEMOCRATIC DECISION** (last resort)
- If issue affects whole group, bring to vote
- Ensure transparency and fairness
- Respect outcome even if you disagree

### Creating a Conflict-Resilient Culture

**Practices:**
- Regular check-ins (how are people feeling?)
- Rotate leadership and roles
- Transparent decision-making
- Clear expectations and accountability
- Celebrate and appreciate each other
- Remember the shared mission

**Ground Rules:**
- Assume good intent
- Speak from "I" statements
- Listen to understand, not to respond
- Disagree with ideas, not people
- Step up/step back (make space for others)
- What's shared here stays here (confidentiality)

## 2.1 TCAP 101: OVERVIEW FOR TENANTS

### What is TCAP?

**TCAP (Tenant Co-op Acquisition Program)** is a New York City program that helps tenants buy their buildings and become collective owners through **limited-equity cooperatives**.

Instead of paying rent to a landlord who may neglect the building, your rent subsidy (Section 8, CityFHEPS, HASA) helps you buy **shares** in your building. You become a **member-owner** with a voice in how the building is run.

### How Does It Work?

**Step 1:** Tenants organize and form a tenant association  
**Step 2:** Association gets certified by HPD  
**Step 3:** Building is evaluated for eligibility  
**Step 4:** Subsidies are converted to equity contributions  
**Step 5:** Association gets financing to purchase building  
**Step 6:** Cooperative is formed and tenants become owners  
**Step 7:** Ongoing support and oversight from HPD

### What's a Limited-Equity Cooperative?

**Cooperative =** Residents own the building together as a corporation. You own **shares**, not your individual apartment.

**Limited-Equity =** When you sell your shares, you can't charge any price you want. Resale prices are **capped** to keep apartments affordable for future low-income families.

**Benefits:**
‚úÖ Build wealth through equity accumulation  
‚úÖ Democratic control (you elect the board)  
‚úÖ Stability (no landlord can sell building under you)  
‚úÖ Better maintained (you control repairs)  
‚úÖ Community ownership  

**Trade-offs:**
‚ö†Ô∏è Responsibilities (you help run the building)  
‚ö†Ô∏è Limited resale profit (to preserve affordability)  
‚ö†Ô∏è Requires participation and governance  

### Who Is Eligible?

**Buildings:**
- 40%+ of tenants have subsidies
- History of neglect or code violations
- Financially feasible to convert

**Tenants:**
- Receiving housing subsidies (Section 8, CityFHEPS, HASA)
- Income at or below 80% Area Median Income
- Willing to participate in cooperative governance

### What Will It Cost?

**During Conversion:** Your out-of-pocket costs should NOT increase. Your subsidy is redirected, not reduced.

**After Conversion:** You'll pay "carrying charges" (like rent, but you're an owner). This covers:
- Building maintenance and repairs
- Property taxes
- Utilities (if included)
- Reserve fund contributions
- Staff (if applicable)

**Your subsidy continues** to help cover these costs based on your income.

### What About Tenants Who Don't Want to Participate?

**You can stay as a renter!** Participation is 100% voluntary. If you don't want to become a co-op member, you can continue renting with your subsidy. You **will not be evicted**.

### How Long Does It Take?

**Typical Timeline:**
- Organizing and certification: 6-12 months
- Financing and acquisition: 12-18 months
- **Total:** 18-30 months from start to ownership

### Types of Housing Ownership

| Type | You Own | Monthly Payment | Can You Sell? | Who Controls? |
|------|---------|-----------------|---------------|---------------|

### Common Challenges and Solutions

**Challenge:** "People don't come to meetings."  
**Solution:** Make meetings accessible, interesting, and empowering. Provide food, childcare, and interpretation. Rotate times. Celebrate wins.

**Challenge:** "We can't afford repairs."

**Challenge:** "Some members don't pay on time."  
**Solution:** Clear policies with grace periods and payment plans. Financial assistance fund for hardship cases. Legal remedies as last resort.

### Module 2: Building and Maintaining Reserves

**Why Reserves Matter:**

Without reserves, when something breaks (and it will!), you face:
- Special assessments (emergency fees to all members)
- Taking on debt at high interest rates
- Deferring repairs, leading to bigger problems

**Healthy reserve = financial stability**

**Rule of Thumb:** Reserve fund should equal 50-100% of annual operating budget.

**How to Build Reserves:**
1. Include reserve contributions in monthly carrying charges ($50-100/unit/month)
2. Allocate any surplus at year-end to reserves
3. Apply for grants from city/state programs
4. Refinance debt at lower interest rates, put savings into reserves

**Reserve Study:**
Every 5 years, conduct a "reserve study" - professional assessment of:
- What will need replacement/repair in next 10-20 years
- How much it will cost
- Whether your reserves are adequate

### Module 3: Understanding Subsidies and Income Changes

**Subsidy Recertification:**

If you receive a housing subsidy, you must **recertify annually** with:
- Proof of income
- Household composition
- Citizenship/eligible immigration status (for some programs)

**What Happens If My Income Changes?**

**Income goes UP:**
- Your subsidy may decrease or end (based on program rules)
- You may transition to paying full carrying charges
- Limited-equity co-ops have affordability restrictions to keep you housed

**Income goes DOWN:**
- Your subsidy may increase
- You may qualify for additional assistance
- Financial hardship policies may offer payment plans

**Communication is key:** Tell your co-op treasurer and subsidy administrator about changes ASAP.

---

### Module 4: Credit Building and Asset Accumulation

**Cooperative Ownership Builds Wealth:**

Unlike renting, cooperative ownership allows you to:
- **Accumulate equity** in your shares
- **Build credit** (if mortgage payments reported to credit bureaus)
- **Pass assets** to children/family
- **Access home equity** for emergencies (in some co-ops)

**Resale Restrictions Preserve Affordability:**

In a **limited-equity co-op**, when you sell your shares, the resale price is capped. This ensures the next family can also afford to live there.

**Example Formula:**
```
Resale Price = Purchase Price + (Annual Increase √ó Years Owned)
Annual Increase = 2% OR Area Median Income increase, whichever is less
```

**Example:**
- You bought shares for $20,000
- You lived there 10 years
- Resale price = $20,000 + ($400/year √ó 10) = $24,000
- You built $4,000 in wealth!

This is modest compared to market-rate appreciation, BUT:
‚úÖ You had stable, affordable housing  
‚úÖ You built wealth instead of paying rent to a landlord  
‚úÖ You helped preserve affordability for the next family

## 2.4 GOVERNANCE AND DEMOCRACY TRAINING

### Democratic Decision-Making Models

**Consensus:**
- Goal: Everyone agrees (or can live with decision)
- Process: Discussion until agreement reached
- Pros: Inclusive, builds buy-in
- Cons: Can be slow, vulnerable to blocking

**Majority Vote:**
- Goal: More than 50% support
- Process: Proposals, discussion, vote
- Pros: Efficient, clear outcomes
- Cons: Can create winners/losers, alienate minority

**Supermajority:**
- Goal: 2/3 or 3/4 support for major decisions
- Process: Higher threshold for significant changes
- Pros: Ensures broad support for big decisions
- Cons: Can make change difficult

**Recommended: Hybrid Approach**
- Routine decisions: Majority vote
- Significant decisions (budgets, assessments, bylaw changes): Supermajority
- Strive for consensus when possible, fall back on voting when needed

---

### Running Effective Board Meetings

**Before Meeting:**
- Prepare agenda
- Distribute materials in advance
- Set date/time at least 7 days ahead

**During Meeting:**
- Follow agenda
- Use consent agendas for routine items (all approved together)
- Focus board time on strategy and policy, not minutiae
- Record minutes with clear action items

**After Meeting:**
- Distribute minutes within 48 hours
- Follow up on action items
- Report to membership

**Executive Sessions:**
Sometimes board needs private discussion (personnel, legal matters). This is okay, but:
- Use sparingly
- Never make decisions in executive session - only discuss, then vote in open session
- Be transparent about why going into executive session

---

### Ethical Leadership

**Conflict of Interest:**

You have a conflict of interest when you (or family) might personally benefit from a co-op decision.

**Examples:**
- You own a business bidding for co-op contract
- Your relative wants to buy a vacant unit
- You're behind on carrying charges and board is discussing payment policies

**What to Do:**
1. **Disclose** the conflict to the board and members
2. **Recuse** yourself from discussion and vote
3. **Document** in meeting minutes

**Never:**
- Vote on matters benefiting you personally
- Use co-op resources for personal gain
- Accept kickbacks from vendors
- Share confidential information for personal benefit

## 6.5 LONG-TERM SUSTAINABILITY PLANNING

### 10-Year Strategic Planning Framework

**Year 1-2: Stabilization**
- Focus: Governance, financial systems, immediate repairs
- Goal: Achieve baseline stability

**Year 3-5: Growth**
- Focus: Build reserves, capital improvements, strengthen governance
- Goal: Become self-sufficient, reduce TA dependency

**Year 6-10: Leadership**
- Focus: Long-term planning, mentorship, policy advocacy
- Goal: Model for others, advocate for broader change

---

### Succession Planning

**Developing Next Generation Leaders:**

- Mentorship programs
- Youth engagement (teenage residents)
- Leadership training for new members
- Rotate responsibilities
- Document institutional knowledge
- Celebrate and appreciate outgoing leaders

**Avoiding Burnout:**
- Distribute work across multiple people
- Recognize and appreciate volunteers
- Take breaks and celebrate wins
- Access to mental health and self-care resources
- Know when to ask for help

---

### Continuous Improvement

**Annual "Health Check":**

Ask yourselves:
- Are we meeting our mission?
- Is governance working well?
- Are finances healthy?
- Is building well-maintained?
- Are members engaged and satisfied?
- What could we improve?

**Learning from Others:**
- Join NYC Cooperative Coalition (when formed)
- Attend NAHC conferences
- Visit other cooperatives
- Read case studies
- Stay connected to TCAP network
