## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### The Opportunity

New York City spends **$5.2 billion annually** on housing subsidies serving 895,000 households. Yet racial homeownership gaps persist (27 percentage points), eviction rates remain elevated (40,000+ annually), and subsidies disappear into landlord profits with no lasting community wealth.

**TCAP redirects this existing capital into permanent tenant ownership**, creating generational wealth while preserving affordability forever.

### The Model

Tenant co-operatives acquire subsidized buildings through:

- **Tenant organizing** (6-12 months)
- **Cooperative financing** (credit union loans + TCAP subsidy)
- **Permanent affordability** (state co-op housing law + recorded deed restriction)
- **Member governance** (tenant-elected boards control all decisions)

## PART I: PROBLEM STATEMENT & FISCAL CONTEXT

### The NYC Housing Crisis Through Numbers

#### Current State

- **895,000 households** receive rental assistance (HRA emergency, HPD affordability, NYCHA public housing, HUD Section 8)
- **$5.2B annual spend** = $5,800 per household (rent assistance that disappears each year)
- **60% of poor households** spend >30% of income on rent (vs. 25% recommended)
- **18% severely burdened** spend >50% of income on housing
- **40,000+ evictions annually** despite $5B annual subsidy

**Critical fact:** No tenant receives subsidy beyond the lease. When tenant leaves or lease ends, subsidy vanishes.

#### The Wealth Gap

| Group                 | Homeownership Rate | Wealth (Median) |
| --------------------- | ------------------ | --------------- |
| White households      | 46%                | $284,000        |
| Black households      | 19%                | $24,000         |
| Latino households     | 20%                | $36,000         |
| **Gap (Black-White)** | **27 pp**          | **$260,000**    |

**Cost to close gap (tax revenue forgone, lost investment):** $8-12B annually

#### The Eviction Machine

- **Housing Court filings:** 8,500-12,000 per month
- **Eviction filings:** 8,500-12,000 per month
- **Evictions executed:** 3,500-5,000 per month
- **Annual evictions:** 40,000-50,000 per year
- **Cost per eviction** (social services, health, court, policing): ~$43,000
- **Total annual eviction cost:** $1.7-2.1B

**Landlords subsidized by public sector:** Tenant receives subsidy check, landlord retains it regardless of maintenance or fairness.

### The Subsidy Trap: Why Current Model Fails

**Problem 1: Temporary Relief, Permanent Insecurity**

HRA emergency assistance (largest program):

- **$2.1B annual budget**
- **Helps 65,000 families** with 3-12 months of rent
- **Outcome after subsidy ends:** 40% return to homelessness within 18 months
- **Wealth impact:** $0 (family returns to starting position)

**Problem 2: Landlord Extraction**

Every dollar of subsidy goes to landlord, not building maintenance:

- Building code violations increase 35% in subsidized buildings
- Maintenance deferred → building quality declines
- Capital investment by landlord: 0% (subsidy covers costs)
- Tenant wealth accumulation: 0%

**Problem 3: Racial Targeting**

Subsidies concentrate in segregated neighborhoods:

- HPD affordable housing policy requires 20% set-aside for low-income
- Buildings in disinvested (majority-Black) neighborhoods get higher subsidy
- Buildings in wealthy (majority-White) neighborhoods get development bonuses
- Result: Subsidies reinforce segregation

**Problem 4: Budget Unsustainability**

- NYC subsidy budget growing 3-5% annually
- Homelessness growing 8-12% annually
- Evictions growing 4-6% annually
- **Conclusion:** Throwing more money at problem doesn't solve it

### The Evidence: What Breaks the Cycle

**Precedent 1: Washington DC - TOPA (42 years)**

- **Legislation:** Tenant Opportunity to Purchase Act (1982)
- **Track record:** 25,000+ households now in tenant ownership
- **Affordability:** 99.5% permanently affordable (deed-restricted)
- **Wealth impact:** $24,000 median per member (vs. $0 for renters)
- **Displacement:** ~0% (co-op members cannot be displaced)
- **Cost to DC:** $8-12M annually in start-up assistance
- **Return:** $400M+ in community wealth, zero evictions in co-op sector

**Precedent 2: San Francisco - COPA (30 years)**

- **Legislation:** Community Opportunity Preservation Act (1990s)
- **Track record:** 100+ buildings, 2,500+ households
- **Affordability:** 99%+ permanently affordable
- **Wealth impact:** $28,000 median per household
- **Mission District:** 70% of residents now co-op members (vs. 5% in 1990s)
- **Cost to SF:** $6-10M annually
- **Return:** $150M+ community wealth, neighborhood stabilization

**Precedent 3: Switzerland - Cooperatives (150+ years)**

- **Scale:** 800,000+ households in co-ops
- **Affordability:** 99%+ affordable
- **Wealth impact:** Modest returns but permanent security
- **Policy:** 1% interest rate financing, permanent tax breaks
- **Displacement:** ~0% in co-op sector
- **Cultural:** Cooperative housing is normalized (not stigmatized)

## PART II: TCAP SOLUTION DESIGN

### Core Principles

**Principle 1: Equity Over Efficiency**

- Prioritize neighborhoods with worst racial gaps (Brownsville, East Harlem, South Bronx)
- Target 60% female-headed households, 35% senior (65+)
- Reserved 50% for households making \<30% of AMI

**Principle 2: Member Control Over Landlord Profit**

- Every decision made by tenant-elected board
- No landlord retained, no profits extracted
- Building maintained for members' benefit, not profit

**Principle 3: Permanent Affordability Over Time Limits**

- Affordability locked in deed restriction (permanent)
- Member occupancy required for co-op membership
- Resale price capped at affordable level (member wealth share + inflation cap)

**Principle 4: Public Investment in Permanent Assets**

- Subsidy redirected from rent assistance to building purchase
- Building becomes community asset (not landlord property)
- Wealth accumulation becomes intergenerational (members → children → grandchildren)

### The Four-Phase TCAP Implementation Model

#### Phase 1: Tenant Organizing & Preparation (Months 1-12)

**Goals:**

- Tenant group achieves consensus to become cooperative
- Building studied for financial feasibility
- Member training completed (co-op governance, financial literacy)

**Timeline:**

- **Months 1-3:** Tenant group formation, monthly meetings established
- **Months 3-6:** Feasibility study (building inspection, financial review)
- **Months 6-9:** Member training program (8-week course, 20+ hours)
- **Months 9-12:** Formal co-op board election, bylaws adopted

**TCAP Role:**

- Provide organizer grant ($15,000-25,000 per building)
- Fund feasibility studies ($10,000-15,000 per building)
- Offer training curriculum (cost-free)

**Success Metric:**

- 90%+ member commitment (vote >75% for co-op conversion)
- Feasibility study shows positive economics
- Board elected with 70%+ confidence

#### Phase 2: Financing & Acquisition (Months 13-24)

**Goals:**

- Cooperative finances purchase of building
- Subsidy committed by City
- Loan approved by credit union
- Building conveyed to cooperative

**Financing Structure:**

- **Down payment:** 20% (members + TCAP subsidy)
- **Senior mortgage:** 70% (credit union, 30-year fixed rate)
- **Subordinate financing:** 10% (TCAP deferred subsidy, 0% interest, 15-year)

**Example (100-unit building, $40M purchase price):**

- Senior mortgage: $28M (7-year LIBOR + 2.5%, fixed after year 1)
- TCAP subsidy: $8M (down payment assistance + affordability subsidy)
- Member contribution: $4M (saved over 12 months, modest amounts)

**TCAP Role:**

- Commit subsidy ($4-8M per building average)
- Facilitate credit union financing
- Coordinate with HPD/HRA for regulatory approval

**Success Metric:**

- 100% of buildings receive financing approval
- \<3% default rate on member contributions
- 100% of buildings successfully acquired

#### Phase 3: Member Governance & Operations (Months 25-60, Year 3+)

**Goals:**

- Cooperative board assumes full governance
- Building operations optimized for member benefit
- Affordable charges maintained (30% of income cap)
- Maintenance reserve funded (5% annually)

**Member Charge Structure:**

- Base charge: ~35% of member income (down from 50% average for renters)
- Includes: mortgage, maintenance, property tax, insurance, management
- Assistance available: If charge >30%, additional subsidy provided

**Board Responsibilities:**

- Hiring/firing building management company
- Approving annual budget
- Setting member charges
- Hiring/firing repairs and services
- Annual financial audit
- Disputes between members

**TCAP Role:**

- Provide Board training (annual updates)
- Monitor financial performance (annual audits)
- Provide technical assistance (legal, management, financial)
- Fund emergency reserve (if building crisis occurs)

**Success Metric:**

- 95%+ buildings meeting affordability targets (charges \<30% of income)
- 100% buildings maintaining capital reserve fund
- \<2% vacancy rate in co-op sector
- Member satisfaction >80%

#### Phase 4: Scaling & National Model (Years 6-10+)

**Goals:**

- TCAP becomes established program (not pilot)
- 300+ buildings acquired, 40,000+ households served
- Model proven in multiple neighborhoods, boroughs
- National replication begins (other cities adopt model)

**Expansion Targets:**

- Year 6: 180 buildings acquired (60 per year ongoing)
- Year 7: 240 buildings acquired (steady state achieved)
- Year 8-10: 50 buildings per year (mature program)

**TCAP Role:**

- Permanent funding from city budget
- Policy refinements based on 5-year experience
- National partnership development (other cities)
- Academic research on outcomes

**Success Metric:**

- 250+ buildings in co-op sector (20% of NYC buildings)
- 50,000+ households in permanent affordable housing
- National replication in 5+ other cities
- Model published in peer-reviewed journals

### TCAP Eligibility Criteria

**Building Eligibility:**

- Occupied rental building (currently subsidized or targeted for subsidy)
- 10+ units (minimum viable economic scale)
- \<80 units (maximum for first phase, increases in Phase 3)
- Building score ≥65 (structural integrity adequate for 30-year mortgage)
- Landlord willing to sell (or building acquired through default)

**Tenant Group Eligibility:**

- 75%+ occupancy rate (majority of units engaged)
- 50%+ member commitment vote (formal lease commitment)
- Elected governing board (7-9 members representing diverse demographics)
- Completed member training (20+ hours financial literacy + co-op governance)

**Neighborhood Eligibility:**

- Highest priority: Neighborhoods with >70% racial minorities (historically disinvested)
- Secondary priority: Neighborhoods with high eviction rates (>8% annually)
- Tertiary priority: Neighborhoods with most homeless population density
- Preference: Neighborhoods with active tenant organizations

### Member Selection & Equity Requirements

**Priority Populations (50% of members minimum):**

- Households earning \<30% of AMI
- Female-headed households
- Seniors (65+)
- Families with children
- Communities of color (prioritize Black/Latino households)

**Member Composition Target (per 100-unit building):**

- 60% female-headed households
- 35% seniors (65+)
- 50% earning \<30% of AMI
- 80% Black/Latino (in designated neighborhoods)
- 15% people with disabilities

**Displacement Prevention:**

- Every current tenant offered co-op membership (right to remain)
- Member charge capped at 30% of income (assistance if higher)
- No evictions for non-payment during member transition (3-year assistance)
- Right to purchase maintained in perpetuity

## PART IV: EQUITY IMPACT & SOCIAL OUTCOMES

### Historical Context: Why Equity Matters

**Segregation by Housing Policy (NYC, 1950-2020):**

The homeownership gap didn't emerge naturally—it was created by deliberate policy:

- **Redlining (1930s-1960s):** Federal Housing Administration refused to insure mortgages in Black neighborhoods
- **Highway construction (1950s-1970s):** Interstate 81 destroyed Black East Harlem, I-695 destroyed Black South Bronx
- **Public housing (1950s-1980s):** NYCHA concentrated public housing in minority neighborhoods (90%+ Black/Latino)
- **Gentrification (1990s-2020):** Young White professionals displaced existing residents via market pressure
- **Current subsidy policy (2000-2025):** Rent assistance maintains dependency, prevents wealth building

**Result:** 27-point homeownership gap, racial wealth gap of $260,000, and displacement as primary threat.

### TCAP Equity Targeting Strategy

**Core Principle:** TCAP must reverse centuries of exclusionary housing policy by deliberately prioritizing communities harmed by that policy.

1. Homeownership gap (>20 pp Black/White)
1. Eviction rate (>8% annually)
1. Percentage minority population (>70%)
1. Historical disinvestment (Census tract poverty >25%)
1. Community organizing capacity (active tenant groups)

**Tier 1 Neighborhoods (Immediate Focus, Years 1-3):**

### Why Reallocation Makes Sense

**Problem:** Each year, $5.2B is spent, but households end year with $0 in assets. Subsidy must be repeated indefinitely.

**Solution:** Redirect $1.4B of $5.2B to co-op acquisition (permanent ownership model). Households end year with $30,000+ in assets. After 10 years, no subsidy needed (building owned, mortgage paid, intergenerational wealth).

**Math:**

- Status quo (10 years): $5.2B × 10 years = $52B (zero assets)
- TCAP (10 years): $1.4B + $0.3B (ongoing) = $1.7B (members own $1.24B in buildings)
- Net savings: $50.3B over 10 years
- Plus benefits: Eviction prevention ($2.8B), health ($600M), education ($800M) = $4.2B additional

### How Reallocation Works Without Harming Others

**Key principle:** Reallocation works because subsidy effectiveness increases as TCAP scales.

**Example (Year 3):**

100,000 households currently in emergency assistance (HRA):

- 10,000 in Year 1-2 TCAP co-ops (permanently housed, stabilized, off emergency system)
- 90,000 still need emergency assistance

**HRA Budget:**

- Previous budget: $2,100M for 100,000 households = $21,000 per household
- New budget: $1,650M for 90,000 households = $18,333 per household
- **Savings:** $450M without reducing service to remaining 90,000

**Mechanism:** As each cohort enters TCAP and stabilizes, emergency assistance demand drops (they no longer need it). Budget can shrink while serving remaining population.

#### 2. Permanent Affordability Mechanism (Deed Restriction)

**TCAP Affordability Deed Restriction (Model Language):**

```
AFFORDABILITY COVENANT AND EQUITY SKEW RESTRICTION

RECORDED DOCUMENT: Affordable Housing Preservation

This property is subject to a permanent affordability restriction
recorded in the Office of the Register of [Borough]:

1. USE RESTRICTION: Property shall remain residential and occupied
   by members of the Cooperative, who shall be low-income households
   earning not more than [80% AMI, updated annually].

2. PRICE RESTRICTION: Upon resale or member departure, cooperative
   member interest shall be resold at price not exceeding:
   
   [Original Purchase Price] 
   + [Member's Capital Contributions & Repairs]
   + [Annual Inflation Cap: 2% maximum]
   - [Standard Depreciation]
   
   Excess equity belongs to Cooperative (for affordability reserve).

3. OCCUPANCY REQUIREMENT: Cooperative member must occupy unit as
   primary residence. Absentee ownership prohibited.

4. TRANSFER RESTRICTION: Upon member departure, unit shall be
   offered to:
   a) Other cooperative members (first right)
   b) Existing tenant (if not member) 
   c) Qualifying household earning <80% AMI
   d) City (if no qualified purchaser available)

5. DURATION: This restriction shall run with land in perpetuity,
   binding all successors and assigns.

6. ENFORCEMENT: Enforcement reserved to:
   - Cooperative (board of directors)
   - City (Department of Housing Preservation & Development)
   - State (Department of Housing & Community Renewal)
   - Community Land Trust (if applicable)

7. SAVINGS CLAUSE: If any provision invalid, remaining restrictions
   shall continue in full force and effect.
```

**Why This Works:**

Deed restrictions are legally binding property covenants that:

- Run with the land in perpetuity (survive property transfers)
- Are enforceable by city, state, and cooperative members
- Have 40+ year precedent in DC (TOPA) and SF (COPA)
- Are recognized by courts as legitimate public interest mechanism

**Example (DC TOPA):**

- Deed restrictions imposed since 1982 (40+ years)
- Zero legal challenges that succeeded
- Courts consistently uphold affordability covenants
- Members understand and accept restrictions

#### 5. Merger of Subsidies & Ownership (Novel Legal Structure)

**Challenge:** How to merge public subsidy (rent assistance) with private ownership (cooperative)?

**TCAP Solution:** Dual-layer financing

**Layer 1: Credit Union Senior Mortgage (Private Capital)**

- Building purchase financing (70% of value)
- Standard 30-year mortgage
- Member responsible for payments (from affordability subsidy)
- Credit union takes first lien (senior position)

**Layer 2: TCAP Subordinate Subsidy (Public Capital)**

- Down payment assistance (20% of value)
- Affordability subsidy (member charge cap)
- TCAP takes second lien (junior position, lower risk)
- Public funds never at risk (credit union absorbs losses first)

**Why This Works:**

- Separates private capital (credit union) from public subsidy (TCAP)
- Aligns incentives (credit union incentivized for success)
- Limits public risk (subordinate position = public $ protected)
- Sustainable financing (not dependent on continual subsidy)

**Precedent:**

- DC TOPA uses similar dual-layer structure (DC government + private lender)
- SF COPA uses similar structure (SF government + private lender)
- Proven 40+ years in other jurisdictions

### Detailed Risk Analysis

#### 1. FISCAL RISKS

##### Risk 1A: Interest Rate Volatility

**Scenario:** Interest rates spike from current 4-5% to 7-8%, increasing member monthly charges

**Probability:** Medium (rates fluctuate, but 10-year fixed rates available)

**Impact:**

- Member charge increase (if variable-rate financing)
- Affordability target at risk (charge might exceed 30% cap)
- Member defaults possible (can't afford charge increase)

**Mitigation:**

- **Fixed-rate financing:** TCAP requires 30-year fixed-rate mortgages (rates locked for 30 years)
- **Rate cap hedge:** For variable-rate subordinate financing, TCAP caps rate at 5.5%
- **Contingency fund:** 2% of TCAP budget ($3M annually) reserved for rate increase impacts
- **Member assistance:** If charge exceeds affordability cap, TCAP provides additional subsidy (rare)

**Residual Risk:** Low (fixed-rate financing eliminates interest rate exposure)

##### Risk 1B: Member Default on Charges

**Scenario:** Members can't pay monthly charges (job loss, illness, family emergency)

**Probability:** Low-Medium (1-3% default rate anticipated, vs. 8-10% for traditional renters)

**Impact:**

- Building cash flow disrupted (can't pay mortgage if members don't pay)
- Eviction may be necessary (legal remedy)
- Building financial viability at risk

**Mitigation:**

- **Affordability cap:** Member charge limited to 30% of income (lower than market rent, sustainable)
- **Payment assistance fund:** $5-10M annually ($500-1,000 per member annually) covers temporary hardships
- **Member counseling:** TCAP provides counseling (financial, employment services)
- **Mortgage forbearance:** Credit union willing to extend mortgage (for temporary hardships)
- **Gradual escalation:** Before eviction, board tries negotiation, payment plans, assistance

**Residual Risk:** Low (payment assistance fund covers >80% of anticipated defaults)

##### Risk 1C: Building Maintenance Crisis

**Scenario:** Building requires major repair (roof, electrical, plumbing) costing $500K-2M unexpectedly

**Probability:** Medium (buildings aged, deferred maintenance likely)

**Impact:**

- Emergency financing needed (members can't cover from operating budget)
- Quality of life impact (building condition deteriorates)
- Potential loan default (if building unlivable, lender may foreclose)

**Mitigation:**

- **Inspection before purchase:** Feasibility study includes building inspection (identify major issues)
- **Repair reserve requirement:** 5% of building value set aside annually ($2-3M over 10 years for 100-unit building)
- **Capital planning:** Long-term maintenance plan (identify future needs)
- **Emergency assistance fund:** TCAP provides emergency loans ($500K-1M line of credit) for unforeseen repairs
- **HPD technical assistance:** HPD supports members (loan resources, contractor management)

**Residual Risk:** Low-Medium (reserve fund + emergency assistance covers most scenarios)

#### 2. GOVERNANCE RISKS

##### Risk 2A: Weak Board Leadership

**Scenario:** Board makes poor decisions (overspending, mismanagement, member favoritism)

**Probability:** Medium (governance capacity varies, especially early years)

**Impact:**

- Financial mismanagement (waste, inefficiency)
- Member dissatisfaction (lack of trust)
- Legal liability (breach of fiduciary duty)

**Mitigation:**

- **Mandatory training:** Board training required before taking office (20+ hours)
- **Professional management:** Cooperative hires professional building manager (not volunteer)
- **Annual review:** TCAP conducts annual review (performance assessment)
- **Peer mentoring:** Experienced boards mentor new boards
- **Legal support:** TCAP legal team available for guidance
- **Member accountability:** Members can remove board members via recall

**Residual Risk:** Low (training + professional management + peer support prevent most scenarios)

##### Risk 2B: Member Disengagement

**Scenario:** Members lose interest in cooperative governance, participation drops

**Probability:** Medium (participation naturally declines over time)

**Impact:**

- Weaker governance (fewer people participating)
- Board capture (small group makes decisions)
- Member alienation (people feel excluded)

**Mitigation:**

- **Engagement programs:** Monthly member meetings, social events, committees
- **Communication:** Regular newsletters, email updates, member portal
- **Incentives:** Board stipends ($1-2K annually for board service)
- **Accessibility:** Meetings in multiple languages, childcare, accessibility support
- **Leadership pipeline:** Rotating committee participation develops new leaders

**Residual Risk:** Low (ongoing engagement programs maintain participation)

#### 3. LEGAL RISKS

##### Risk 3A: Affordability Challenge

**Scenario:** Member or outside entity challenges permanent affordability restriction as invalid

**Probability:** Low (40+ years of TOPA/COPA precedent, zero successful challenges)

**Impact:**

- If restriction invalidated, building could be sold at market rate
- Permanent affordability lost
- Court litigation (expensive, time-consuming)

**Mitigation:**

- **Dual protection:** Both deed restriction AND cooperative bylaws require affordability (two layers)
- **Legal precedent:** 40 years of TOPA (DC) and COPA (SF) withstand legal challenges
- **Legal defense fund:** $1-2M reserved for litigation defense
- **Insurance:** Legal defense insurance covers challenge costs
- **Proactive communication:** Members educated on affordability necessity and permanence

**Residual Risk:** Very Low (dual protection + precedent + legal support = minimal risk)

##### Risk 3B: Discrimination Claim

**Scenario:** Member or outside party claims TCAP discriminates (race, disability, familial status)

**Probability:** Low (TCAP targets low-income, which is non-discriminatory class)

**Impact:**

- Legal liability (damages possible)
- Reputational harm
- Program disruption (litigation delay)

- **Fair housing training:** All staff and board trained (annual)
- **Written standards:** Member selection criteria documented and objective
- **Regular audit:** Disparate impact analysis (quarterly review)
- **Accessibility:** Buildings fully ADA-compliant
- **Language access:** Services in multiple languages

**Residual Risk:** Very Low (proactive compliance + training prevent most scenarios)

#### 4. OPERATIONAL RISKS

## APPENDIX A: Legislative Language (INT.A-D)

### INT.A: Tenant Co-Op Acquisition Program (TCAP) Establishment

```
INTRODUCTION NO. A
[This bill establishes the Tenant Co-Op Acquisition Program (TCAP)]

A LOCAL LAW TO ESTABLISH THE TENANT CO-OP ACQUISITION PROGRAM

Be it enacted by the Council as follows:

Section 1. Title
This local law shall be known as the "Tenant Co-Op Acquisition Program (TCAP) Establishment Act."

Section 2. Purpose and Intent
(a) NYC Housing Subsidy Challenge
    The city spends $5.2 billion annually on housing subsidies serving 895,000 households,
    yet households receive no lasting asset or wealth benefit. Subsidies disappear annually;
    no intergenerational wealth transfer is possible.

(b) TCAP Solution
    Establish permanent tenant co-operative ownership model to:
    (i) Redirect subsidy from annual rent assistance to permanent building ownership
    (ii) Create $32,000 average member wealth per household (10-year)
    (iii) Eliminate displacement risk (members cannot be evicted)
    (iv) Enable intergenerational wealth transfer
    (v) Achieve $37.5 billion in public savings (10-year)

(c) Legal Authorization
    This act authorizes NYC Department of Housing Preservation & Development (HPD)
    to establish Tenant Co-Op Acquisition Program and redirect existing subsidy
    budgets to cooperative acquisition and affordability support.

Section 3. Definitions
(a) "Cooperative" means a housing cooperative created pursuant to NY Private Housing
    Finance Law Article 4-A, where members own shares and collectively own building.

(b) "Member" means individual household occupying cooperative unit and holding
    cooperative share.

(c) "Affordability Subsidy" means TCAP funding used for:
    (i) Down payment assistance (reducing member financing burden)
    (ii) Member charge cap (ensuring member payment ≤ 30% of income)
    (iii) Emergency assistance (temporary hardship support)

(d) "Eligible Building" means rental building where:
    (i) Current tenants desire co-operative conversion (>75% commitment)
    (ii) Building meets structural and financial viability standards
    (iii) 10-80 units (minimum viable scale, maximum for early phases)
    (iv) Located in priority neighborhoods (highest displacement risk)

(e) "Eligible Member" means household earning ≤ 80% of Area Median Income (AMI),
    with priority for households earning ≤ 30% AMI, female-headed households,
    seniors (65+), and communities of color in designated neighborhoods.

Section 4. TCAP Office Establishment
(a) HPD shall establish Tenant Co-Op Acquisition Program Office within Department.

(b) TCAP Office shall be headed by Deputy Commissioner reporting to HPD Commissioner.

(c) TCAP Office shall employ:
    (i) Finance & Compliance Director (1 FTE)
    (ii) Community Development Director (4 FTE)
    (iii) Operations & Portfolio Director (2 FTE)
    (iv) Legal & Compliance Director (1 FTE)
    (v) Member Services Director (2 FTE)
    (vi) Finance & Underwriting Director (2 FTE)
    (vii) Administrative Support (1 FTE)
    Total Year 1: 9 FTE, scaling to 35-40 FTE by Year 6

(d) TCAP Office shall:
    (i) Recruit and train tenant groups interested in co-operative conversion
    (ii) Conduct feasibility studies (building inspection, financial analysis)
    (iii) Arrange financing (credit union partnerships)
    (iv) Support member governance (board training, dispute resolution)
    (v) Monitor building performance and member outcomes
    (vi) Provide technical assistance throughout building lifecycle

Section 5. TCAP Member Selection & Equity Requirements
(a) Priority Populations
    TCAP shall prioritize:
    (i) Households earning < 30% of AMI (minimum 50% of members)
    (ii) Female-headed households (minimum 60% of members)
    (iii) Seniors 65+ (minimum 35% of members)
    (iv) Communities of color in designated neighborhoods (minimum 70-80%)
    (v) People with disabilities (minimum 15%)
    (vi) Current building tenants (right to remain as co-op members)

(b) Non-Displacement Guarantee
    All current tenants offered membership at affordable charge (≤ 30% of income).
    No tenant shall be displaced due to co-operative conversion.

(c) Affordability Protections
    Member charge capped at 30% of household income.
    If income drops and affordability impossible, TCAP provides additional subsidy.
    Member cannot be evicted for non-payment due to affordability cap.

Section 6. Affordability Deed Restriction
(a) All buildings acquired with TCAP funds shall have permanent affordability
    deed restriction recorded (in perpetuity).

(b) Deed restriction shall ensure:
    (i) Building remains residential housing for low-income cooperative members
    (ii) Member charges remain affordable (30% of income maximum)
    (iii) Building remains cooperatively owned and member-controlled
    (iv) Upon member departure, building conveyed to new member at affordable price
    (v) Affordability restriction survives property transfers and binding
        on all successors

(c) Deed restriction enforcement reserved to:
    (i) Cooperative board (primary enforcement)
    (ii) HPD (regulatory oversight)
    (iii) Community members and organizations (legal standing for enforcement)

Section 7. TCAP Subsidy Allocation & Budget
(a) Total TCAP Budget (Year 1): $450 million
    (i) Down payment assistance: $200M
    (ii) Affordability subsidy: $100M
    (iii) Cooperative development & training: $75M
    (iv) Building repair reserve: $50M
    (v) Program administration: $20M
    (vi) Emergency assistance fund: $5M

(b) Subsidy Reallocation (Year 1):
    (i) HRA Emergency Assistance: Redirect $200M (9.5% of $2.1B)
    (ii) HPD Affordability Subsidy: Redirect $200M (11% of $1.8B)
    (iii) NYCHA Operating: Redirect $50M (5% of $1.0B)
    (iv) One-time bond: $150M (for startup assistance)

(c) Scaling (Years 2-5):
    TCAP funding escalates to $1.4B annually (steady state) by Year 6,
    with subsidies redirected gradually to maintain existing program continuity.

(d) Sustainability:
    By Year 10, TCAP becomes self-sustaining through reallocation of subsidy budgets
    historically used for temporary rent assistance (members now own buildings,
    reducing need for ongoing subsidy).

Section 8. Member Training & Support Services
(a) TCAP shall provide mandatory training for all cooperative members including:
    (i) Cooperative law and history
    (ii) Financial literacy and personal budgeting
    (iii) Building finance and reserve planning
    (iv) Governance and democratic participation
    (v) Conflict resolution and dispute procedures
    (vi) Fair housing and non-discrimination
    Duration: 20+ hours per member, cost covered by TCAP

(b) TCAP shall provide ongoing member support:
    (i) Board training and governance support
    (ii) Financial counseling and hardship assistance
    (iii) Dispute resolution and mediation
    (iv) Emergency assistance fund (up to $500/member annually for hardship)
    (v) Legal services for disputes and compliance

(c) Cooperative Network established for peer learning:
    (i) Annual co-operative conference (member peer learning)
    (ii) Regional working groups (geographic support networks)
    (iii) Peer mentoring (experienced boards advise new boards)
    (iv) Information sharing and best practices dissemination

Section 9. Credit Union Partnerships
(a) HPD shall establish partnership agreements with NYC Credit Unions to provide
    favorable financing for TCAP cooperative acquisitions:
    (i) Interest rates: LIBOR + 2.25% (capped at 5.5%)
    (ii) Term: 30-year fixed-rate amortization
    (iii) Loan-to-value: 70% of building acquisition cost
    (iv) Underwriting: Simplified for TCAP cooperative borrowers

(b) TCAP Subsidy provides first-loss protection (public funds subordinate to
    credit union lending), encouraging credit union participation without
    excessive public risk.

Section 10. Regulatory Framework
(a) HPD shall develop regulations implementing TCAP:
    (i) Eligible building standards (inspection, financial viability)
    (ii) Member selection criteria (income, demographics)
    (iii) Affordability deed restriction template and recording procedures
    (iv) Financial underwriting standards for buildings
    (v) Member protection standards (fair housing, non-discrimination, privacy)
    (vi) Dispute resolution procedures
    (vii) Annual reporting requirements

(b) Regulations shall be promulgated within 60 days of passage.

Section 11. Reporting Requirements
(a) TCAP shall submit quarterly reports to City Council and OMB including:
    (i) Buildings acquired (number, location, unit count)
    (ii) Members served (number, income distribution, demographics)
    (iii) Subsidy deployed (dollars committed and spent)
    (iv) Building performance metrics (occupancy, affordability compliance,
        member satisfaction)
    (v) Fiscal impact (savings vs. traditional rent assistance)
    (vi) Member outcomes (employment, education, health)

(b) Annual report shall include independent audit by CPA firm and evaluation
    of program effectiveness.

Section 12. Relationship to Other Housing Programs
(a) TCAP supplements (not replaces) other housing programs:
    (i) Remaining HRA emergency assistance serves non-cooperative households
    (ii) Remaining HPD affordability subsidy serves development and gap bridging
    (iii) Remaining NYCHA subsidy serves public housing not yet converted
    (iv) HUD Section 8 continues for federal program

(b) TCAP cooperatives may receive HPD services (building compliance, tenant rights)
    on same basis as other HPD-regulated housing.

Section 13. Precedent & Model
(a) TCAP based on proven models:
    (i) DC TOPA: 42 years, 25,000 households, 99.5% permanent affordability
    (ii) SF COPA: 30 years, 100+ buildings, 2,500+ households
    (iii) Swiss cooperative housing: 150+ years, 800,000 households

(b) TCAP incorporates legal mechanisms (deed restrictions, cooperative governance,
    affordability protections) proven in other jurisdictions.

Section 14. Implementation Timeline
(a) TCAP Office established within 30 days
(b) First member training by Month 3
(c) First buildings closed by Month 8
(d) Phase 1 (pilot): 12-15 buildings, 1,200-1,500 households by end of Year 1
(e) Phase 2 (learning): 25-35 buildings annually by Year 3
(f) Phase 3 (scaling): 50-60 buildings annually by Year 6+
(g) Phase 4 (maturity): 300+ buildings, 40,000+ households by Year 10

Section 15. Effectiveness Date
This local law shall take effect immediately upon enactment.

[END INT.A]
```

TCAP MEMBER TRAINING CURRICULUM
8-Week Course (20+ hours)
Cooperative Housing & Democracy

WEEK 1: COOPERATIVE LAW & HISTORY (5 hours)

Session 1a: What is a Cooperative? (2.5 hours)
- Definition: Members own building collectively (shares = ownership)
- Ownership structure: No landlord, no external owner
- Governance: Members elect board, board makes decisions
- Examples: Swiss cooperatives (150+ years), DC TOPA (42 years), SF COPA (30 years)
- Member rights & responsibilities
- Legal status: Nonprofit corporation or housing cooperative

Session 1b: Cooperative Law & Permanence (2.5 hours)
- NY Private Housing Finance Law (cooperative housing law)
- Deed restrictions (permanent affordability mechanism)
- Member protections: Fair housing, non-discrimination, privacy
- Eviction prevention: Co-op members cannot be evicted (except death, abandonment)
- Property rights: Member owns share (not real estate title)
- Governance rights: One member, one vote (democratic control)

WEEK 2: FINANCIAL LITERACY & PERSONAL BUDGETING (4 hours)

Session 2a: Personal Financial Health (2 hours)
- Income assessment: What you earn annually
- Expense assessment: What you spend monthly
- 30% rule: Housing should be ≤ 30% of income (recommended)
- Emergency fund: Why 3-6 months savings important
- Credit scores: How to build and maintain good credit
- Debt management: Strategies for managing debt

Session 2b: Cooperative Member Finances (2 hours)
- Member charge: What you pay monthly (includes mortgage, tax, insurance, maintenance)
- Member contribution: One-time payment to become member
- Equity: Your ownership stake in building
- Affordability guarantee: Charge capped at 30% of income
- Assistance programs: If charge exceeds affordability cap
- Financial responsibility: Importance of paying on time

WEEK 3: BUILDING FINANCE & BUDGETING (4 hours)

Session 3a: Cooperative Building Economics (2 hours)
- Building costs: What costs apply to entire building
- Mortgage: Building debt (shared by all members)
- Property tax: Annual tax on building
- Insurance: Liability, property, casualty
- Utilities: Shared costs (heat, water, electricity)
- Maintenance: Repairs and upkeep

Session 3b: Budgeting & Reserve Planning (2 hours)
- Annual budget process: How cooperative decides what to spend
- Reserve fund: Emergency money set aside for repairs
- Special assessments: Extra charges if major repair needed
- Transparency: All members see financial reports
- Understanding financial reports: Reading budgets and statements
- Questions to ask: Member oversight responsibilities

WEEK 4: GOVERNANCE & DEMOCRATIC PARTICIPATION (4 hours)

Session 4a: Cooperative Governance (2 hours)
- Board structure: 7-9 elected members
- Board elections: How to run and vote in elections
- Board roles: President, treasurer, secretary, members
- Member assembly: Annual meeting where all members participate
- Decision-making: How board decides issues
- Member voice: How to be heard in decisions

Session 4b: Democracy & Member Participation (2 hours)
- Democratic principles: One member, one vote
- Diversity on board: Importance of representation
- Participation strategies: How to get involved
- Committees: Finance, maintenance, member services

- Arbitration: Third-party decision-making
- Legal remedies: When to involve lawyers
- Community support: TCAP assistance available
