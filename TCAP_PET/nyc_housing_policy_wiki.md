## Overview

New York City's housing policy framework operates through multiple interconnected programs designed to maintain housing affordability while protecting tenant rights and ensuring landlord viability. Unlike many states, New York does not have a single statewide rent control law; instead, the system is managed through a comprehensive rent regulation program created by the **Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act of 2019 (HSTPA)**, effective June 14, 2019.

### Key Statistics (2025)

- **Total NYC rental units affected by regulation:** ~1.1 million
- **Rent-stabilized units:** ~44% of NYC rental market
- **Rent-controlled units:** ~1% of rental market (phased out)
- **Section 8 households in NYC:** ~85,000-120,000 (largest program in the nation)
- **Total rental assistance programs (2024):** 352,000 NYC households

## Key Regulatory Agencies

### 1. **New York State Division of Housing and Community Renewal (DHCR)**

**Jurisdiction:** Statewide rent control and rent stabilization oversight

**Primary Functions:**

- Enforces rent control and rent stabilization laws across New York State
- Administers the Office of Rent Administration (ORA)
- Processes rent overcharge complaints
- Maintains rent regulation databases and registrations
- Sets Maximum Base Rent (MBR) for rent-controlled apartments
- Oversees regulatory compliance and building violations

**Key Regulatory Structure:**

- Divides NYC into geographic rent offices (Manhattan, Bronx, Brooklyn, Queens, Staten Island)
- Requires owners to file annual rent registrations
- Processes applications for Major Capital Improvements (MCIs)
- Administers hardship rent increase requests
- Enforces the Rent Stabilization Code and Tenant Protection Regulations

**Contact & Resources:**

- Website: hcr.ny.gov
- Rent Regulated Building Search (online database)
- Complaint forms: RA-89 (rent overcharge complaint)

### 2. **NYC Rent Guidelines Board (RGB)**

**Jurisdiction:** Sets annual rent adjustment guidelines for rent-stabilized apartments in NYC

**Structure:**

- 9-member board (1 chair, 4 members nominated by landlord organizations, 4 by tenant organizations)
- Created to balance interests of tenants and building owners
- Independent from both the mayor and governor

**Mandate:**

- Establish fair rent increase percentages for one-year and two-year leases
- Consider cost of operating buildings, inflation, and economic conditions
- Apply increases to leases commencing on/after October 1 through September 30 of following year
- Issue annual orders (currently Order 57 for 2025-26 lease year)

**Current Guidelines (2025-2026 Lease Year - Order 57):**

- One-year lease: 3% increase
- Two-year lease: 4.5% increase
- Special guidelines for decontrolled units and special circumstances

**Website:** rentguidelinesboard.cityofnewyork.us

### 3. **NYC Department of Housing Preservation and Development (HPD)**

**Jurisdiction:** City-level housing policy and subsidy administration

**Primary Functions:**

- Administers local rental assistance programs
- Oversees NYC 15/15 rental assistance program
- Manages relationship with DHCR on local issues
- Issues violation and compliance orders
- Maintains building inspection records

**Contact:** nyc.gov/site/hpd

### 4. **NYC Housing Authority (NYCHA)**

**Jurisdiction:** Section 8/Housing Choice Voucher administration

**Primary Functions:**

- Administers approximately 85,000 Housing Choice Vouchers (largest Section 8 program nationally)
- Manages landlord and tenant relationships
- Processes applications, certifications, and inspections
- Coordinates HUD funding and compliance
- Transitioning Emergency Housing Vouchers (EHV) to traditional HCV program

**Key Contact:** NYCHA Self-Service Portal (selfserve.nycha.info)

### 5. **U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD)**

**Jurisdiction:** Federal oversight of Housing Choice Voucher program

**Responsibilities:**

- Allocates federal funding to NYCHA for Section 8 program
- Sets income limits and eligibility standards
- Establishes payment standards
- Oversees program compliance
- Note: Currently sunsetting Emergency Housing Voucher (EHV) program

## Rent Stabilization Program

### Overview & Coverage

**What It Is:** Rent stabilization is a form of rent regulation that limits annual rent increases and provides lease renewal rights to tenants.

**Coverage:** Approximately 970,000 apartments in NYC (44% of rental market)

**Buildings Subject to Rent Stabilization:**

- Buildings with 6 or more units built before January 1, 1974
- Buildings receiving tax benefits (affordable housing programs)
- Properties subject to Rent and Rehabilitation Law (ETPA)
- Specific buildings under regulatory agreements with city/state agencies

**Buildings Exempt from Rent Stabilization:**

- Single-family homes or small buildings (fewer than 6 units)
- Housing built on or after January 1, 1974 (unless tax-benefited)
- Institutional housing (dorms, hospitals, nonprofits)
- Transient housing (hotels, SROs for short-term stays)
- Units not used as tenant's primary residence
- Co-op and condo conversions (existing tenants may retain, new sales exempt)

### Legal Framework

**Primary Statutes:**

- New York State Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act of 2019 (HSTPA)
- New York State Emergency Tenant Protection Act (ETPA) of 1974
- NY Real Property Law Article 26 (Stabilization)
- NYC Administrative Code Title 26, Chapter 5

**Key Provisions:**

- Rents can increase annually only by RGB-approved percentages
- Tenants have right to lease renewal
- Landlords can only refuse renewal under limited "good cause" circumstances
- Surcharges for Individual Apartment Improvements (IAIs) capped at 1/60th of improvement cost
- Major Capital Improvements (MCIs) capped at 1/84th (for leases 1+ years) or 1/120th (leases under 1 year) of cost
- Lease term (1-year or 2-year) affects rent increase amounts
- Service reductions trigger rent reduction obligations

### Rent Increase Mechanisms

**Annual RGB Increases (2025-2026):**

- One-year lease renewal: 3%
- Two-year lease renewal: 4.5%

**Additional Permitted Increases:**

#### Major Capital Improvements (MCIs)

- Eligibility: Building-wide improvements with useful life of 15+ years
- Cost threshold: Typically $3,000+ per unit
- Increase: 1/84th to 1/120th of MCI cost per month
- Process: DHCR approval required; tenant challenge rights exist
- Common examples: New roof, boiler, windows, exterior restoration

#### Individual Apartment Improvements (IAIs)

- Owner-initiated upgrades: Capped at 1/60th of cost monthly
- Tenant-requested improvements: IAI pass-through allowed
- Examples: Hardwood floors, updated kitchen, bathroom renovations

#### Preferential Rent Changes

- Landlord can reduce preferential rent by up to 10% per lease term
- Can eliminate preferential rent within 6 months of lease commencement
- Tenant may challenge in court if reduced due to organizing activities

#### Hardship Rent Increases

- Available under limited circumstances (rare)
- Requires DHCR approval based on demonstrated economic hardship
- Subject to RGB annual increases

### Tenant Protections

**Lease Renewal Rights:**

- Tenant may elect to renew lease on expiration
- Landlord can refuse renewal only if:
  - Owner seeks to occupy unit as primary residence
  - Building is being demolished
  - Substantial rehabilitation planned
  - Owner needs to relocate employee
  - Other specific statutory reasons
- Non-renewal refusals must be in writing with detailed reason
- Tenants can challenge refusals in Housing Court

**Rent Overcharge Protections:**

- Prohibition on rents exceeding legal regulated rent
- Tenant remedies: File complaint with DHCR or pursue court action
- Lookback period: 6 years or, if overcharge due to fraud, 4 years from discovery
- Penalties: Overcharged amount plus interest + treble damages for willful overcharges

**Service Reduction Remedies:**

- Tenant may withhold rent (escrow) if services reduced
- Rent reduced by percentage corresponding to service loss
- Examples: Heat, hot water, painting, janitor services

### Registration & Compliance

**Annual Registration Requirement:**

- Landlords must file rent registration with DHCR annually
- Form filed by September 15 for leases beginning October 1
- Must reflect all legal rent components
- Failure to register can result in:
  - Penalties and interest
  - Rent reduction to default amount
  - Treble damages if willful

**Owner Obligations:**

- Provide legal regulated rent notice before lease commencement
- List all rent components (base + MCI/IAI surcharges)
- Deliver lease at least 30 days before expiration
- Maintain apartment in good repair
- Provide required services
- Comply with building code and housing maintenance standards

### Rent Reduction Causes

Tenants may request rent reduction for:

- Failure to provide essential services (heat, hot water, repairs)
- Housing code violations
- Non-compliance with building maintenance standards
- Violation of lease terms affecting habitability

## Rent Control Program

### Overview

**Status:** Actively declining; covers only ~1% of NYC apartments (largely phased out)

**What It Is:** Traditional rent control (much stricter than rent stabilization) that limits rent increases based on Maximum Base Rent (MBR) system

**Eligibility:** Tenants occupying same apartment continuously since before June 30, 1974

**Application:** Primarily affects "preferential rent" apartments and stabilized units eligible for decontrol

### Legal Framework

**Primary Statutes:**

- New York State Rent Control Law (administered under ETPA legacy)
- Emergency Tenant Protection Act of 1974
- NY Real Property Law Article 26

### Maximum Base Rent (MBR) System

**How MBR Works:**

- DHCR determines a maximum base rent and maximum collectible rent for each controlled apartment
- Adjustments made every two years by DHCR
- Based on assessment of building operating costs + reasonable profit
- Building owners must apply for MBR adjustment (Form RA-28)
- Tenants can challenge adjustments based on:
  - Building has violations
  - Owner expenses don't warrant increase
  - Service reductions

**2026-27 MBR Application:**

- Owners must file and receive DHCR Order of Eligibility
- Use Owner Rent Regulation Application (ORRA) online system
- Expedited approval for buildings with zero HPD violations

### Tenant Protections Under Rent Control

- Stricter limits on rent increases than rent stabilization
- Lease renewal rights similar to stabilization
- Enhanced service protection provisions
- Greater challenge rights to owner-requested increases

### Decontrol & Phase-Out

**Decontrol Restrictions (2019 Amendments):**

- Eliminated vacancy bonus (previous mechanism for decontrol)
- Eliminated high-rent decontrol provisions
- All rent-controlled apartments now continue in regulation unless:
  - Tenant voluntarily vacates
  - Tenant becomes ineligible (moves out, loses occupancy)
  - Legal decontrol exceptions apply

**Decontrol Special Guidelines:**

- Special rent guidelines apply to units decontrolled after September 30, 2025
- Permissible increase: 49% above maximum base rent for initial decontrolled lease

## Section 8 Housing Choice Voucher Program

### Program Overview

**What It Is:** Federal rental subsidy program created by Housing and Community Development Act of 1978

**National Scope:** Over 5 million households nationally receive Housing Choice Vouchers

**NYC Administration:** NYCHA administers ~85,000 vouchers (largest program in the nation)

**NYC Participant Numbers:**

- Voucher holders: ~120,000 households
- Landlords participating: Over 25,000
- Demographics: ~40% children, ~16% age 61+, ~25% disabled

### Eligibility Requirements

**Income Limits (2025):**

- Determined by household size and NYC area limits
- Set at approximately 50% of Area Median Income (AMI)
- Examples (family of 4, approximate):
  - Gross annual income limit: ~$63,650
  - Monthly gross income: ~$5,304

**Family Size Income Limits:**

- 1 person: ~$37,500
- 2 persons: ~$42,900
- 3 persons: ~$48,250
- 4 persons: ~$53,600
- 5 persons: ~$57,950
- 6 persons: ~$62,300
- 7 persons: ~$66,650
- 8+ persons: Additional increment

**Other Requirements:**

- Valid SSN (for all household members)
- Background check (criminal history reviewed)
- No history of lease violations
- Monthly income certification

**Note:** Income must be verified at time of voucher issuance; income changes after issuance don't affect eligibility

### Program Structure & Operation

**Participant Cost Structure:**

- Tenant pays: 30-40% of adjusted monthly income toward rent
- NYCHA pays: Housing Assistance Payment (HAP) to landlord
- Payment formula: Rent - tenant contribution = NYCHA payment

**Housing Quality Standards:**

- Effective January 1, 2025: Transitioning to National Standard for Physical Inspection (NSPIRE)
- Unit must pass inspection before lease commencement and annually
- Standards cover: Safety, maintenance, utilities, accessibility
- Inspections conducted by NYCHA or authorized inspectors

### Landlord Requirements & Process

**Lease & Agreement Process:**

1. Landlord and tenant negotiate lease terms (within program parameters)
1. Unit must meet Housing Quality Standards
1. Lease submitted to NYCHA for approval
1. NYCHA issues Housing Assistance Payment (HAP) contract
1. Landlord receives monthly payment from NYCHA + tenant contribution

**Payment Process (2025):**

- NYCHA receives HUD funding first business day of month
- NYCHA processes landlord payments within one business day of receipt
- Payment includes: Rent minus tenant share
- Payment standard caps maximum rent NYCHA will cover

**Landlord Responsibilities:**

- Maintain unit in compliance with lease and program requirements
- Perform needed repairs promptly
- Maintain habitability standards
- Cooperate with inspections
- Provide lease-required services
- Accept tenant's Section 8 funding as partial/full rent

### Tenant Requirements & Obligations

**Participant Obligations:**

- Complete annual certification (household composition, income, assets)
- Accommodate NYCHA inspections (initial and annual)
- Allow landlord to make necessary repairs
- Comply with lease terms
- Report household composition changes
- Maintain home in good condition
- Pay rent share directly to landlord

**Failure to Comply Consequences:**

- Lease termination
- Eviction
- Removal from program
- Ineligibility for future vouchers

### Waitlist & Application Status (2025)

**Current Status:**

- Waitlist opened: June 3, 2024
- Application period closed: June 9, 2024, 11:59 PM
- Waitlist established: August 1, 2024
- **TEMPORARY PAUSE (effective August 1, 2025):**
  - NYCHA temporarily pausing voucher issuance to general HCV applicants
  - Reason: Transitioning Emergency Housing Vouchers (EHV) to traditional HCV program
  - EHV holders (~5,500 NYC households) migrating to HCV
  - General applicants returned to original waitlist position
  - Timeline: Pending changes in federal funding context

**How Voucher Issuance Works:**

- Applicants called off waitlist in order of application date + priority status
- Priority given to homeless, displaced, holding eviction notices
- Issuance paused pending EHV transition completion

### Rental Assistance Payment Coordination with Rent Regulation

**Key Policy Issue (Resolved 2022):**

- Conflict between rent-stabilized legal regulated rent and HAP contract rent
- Solution: December 2022 legislation (S7235) amended Private Housing Finance Law

**Current Law:**

- Owners of affordable housing properties with regulatory agreements may collect full HAP contract rent
- Even if property subject to rent stabilization
- After subsidy terminates, owner must default to legal regulated rent
- Regulatory agencies must audit compliance every 3 years
- Owners liable for rent overcharges if fail to default to regulated rent after subsidy ends

**Applicable Programs:**

- Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers
- NYC 15/15 local rental assistance
- State/municipal subsidy programs

## Additional Rental Assistance Programs

### NYC 15/15 Rental Assistance Program

**Overview:**

- Local NYC rental assistance program (not federal)
- Administered by NYC Department of Housing Preservation and Development (HPD)
- Provides direct payment to landlords for eligible low-income households

**Subsidy Interaction:**

- Subject to 2022 legislation allowing full contract rent under regulatory agreements
- Must comply with rent stabilization limits after subsidy expires

### SCRIE (Senior Citizen Rent Increase Exemption)

**Eligibility:**

- Primary income from Social Security
- Income not exceeding legal limits
- Age 62 or older
- Occupancy in same rent-stabilized apartment for 20+ years

**Benefits:**

- Rent increase exemption when increase would exceed income increase
- Rent capped at percentage of total income

### DRIE (Disability Rent Increase Exemption)

**Eligibility:**

- Household member certified severely disabled/disabled
- Income at or below specific limits
- Occupancy in same rent-stabilized apartment 20+ years

**Benefits:**

- Similar to SCRIE; exemption from rent increases beyond income growth

### Emergency Rental Assistance (ERA)

**Status (2025):**

- Existing EHV program sunsetting
- NYCHA transitioning ~5,500 EHV households to traditional Section 8 HCV
- Provides emergency housing assistance for vulnerable populations
- Federal program through HUD; administered locally by NYCHA

### Shelter Allowance

**Overview:**

- Component of NYC public assistance system
- Provides shelter allowance for emergency assistance recipients
- Administered through NYC Department of Social Services

## Recent Legislative Changes (2024-2025)

### Good Cause Eviction Law (GCEL) - April 2024

**Scope:**

- Applies to market-rate (non-regulated) tenants in NYC
- Provides first statutory protection for market-rate tenants
- Phases adopted in NYC; other NY municipalities may opt-in

**Key Provisions:**

**Reasonable Rent Increase Standard:**

- Rents cannot increase above "local rent standard"
- Calculation: CPI + 5%, maximum 10% annually
- 2025 local rent standard: 8.79% (based on 3.79% inflation)
- Above-standard increases considered "unreasonable" and grounds for eviction challenges

**Notice Requirements:**

- Landlord must attach Good Cause Eviction notice to:
  - Initial lease
  - Lease renewals
  - Non-renewal notices
  - Rent demand notices exceeding 5%
- Failure to provide notice may invalidate eviction

**Rent Increase Notices:**

- Landlords must provide written notice for increases above 5%
- Notice must disclose local rent standard
- 30-day notice period (for leases under 1 year)
- Longer notice for multi-year leases

**Eviction Protections:**

- Landlord must have "good cause" to evict non-payment tenants
- Cannot evict for rent increase-induced non-payment if increase exceeds local rent standard
- Tenant can challenge eviction in court based on unreasonable increase

**Compliance for Landlords (2025):**

- Ensure rent increases comply with local rent standard
- Provide proper Good Cause notices
- Track annual increases for compliance audits
- Maintain documentation of basis for increases

### 2025 Landlord Requirement Changes

**Application Fee Cap:**

- Application fees capped at $20 maximum
- Cannot exceed actual cost of processing

**Bounced Check Fees:**

- Limited to amount bank charged landlord
- Cannot profit from returned checks
- Prevents fee inflation

**Last Month's Rent Prohibition:**

- Landlords cannot require last month's rent with security deposit at move-in
- Applies to new tenancy agreements
- Protects tenant liquidity at lease commencement

### Rent Guidelines Board Order 57 (2025-2026 Lease Year)

**Approved Increases:**

- One-year lease: 3%
- Two-year lease: 4.5%
- Special guideline for decontrolled units: 49% above MBR

**Board's Rationale:**

- Economic uncertainty reflected in testimony
- Balancing cost of building operation with housing affordability
- Two-year lease increase restarted rulemaking for greater flexibility

### Emergency Housing Voucher (EHV) Program Sunset

**Timeline & Impact:**

- HUD announced EHV program ending (March 6, 2025)
- EHV provided critical support since 2021 (American Rescue Plan)
- NYC impact: ~5,500 households losing program-specific support
- NYCHA mitigation: Transition EHV holders to traditional HCV program
- Federal provision: Public housing authorities allowed to convert EHV to HCV

**Status:**

- NYCHA beginning EHV-to-HCV conversion (August 2025)
- Transition aims to preserve affordability access
- EHV households maintaining rental subsidies through traditional Section 8
- Potential ongoing federal uncertainty regarding HCV funding

### How to File Complaints & Requests

**Rent Overcharge Complaint (DHCR):**

1. Complete RA-89 form
1. Attach supporting documentation (leases, payment records)
1. File with appropriate DHCR Rent Office (geographic location)
1. Processing time: 30-90+ days depending on complexity
1. Looback: 6 years (4 years for fraud cases)

**MCI/IAI Challenge:**

1. File with DHCR within 30 days of lease delivery
1. Complete appropriate registration challenge form
1. Provide evidence supporting challenge (contractor estimates, comparables)
1. DHCR administrative hearing scheduled
1. Right to appeal unfavorable DHCR decision

**Service Reduction Claim:**

1. Provide written notice to landlord of specific service loss
1. Request rent reduction calculation
1. If denied, file complaint with DHCR
1. May withhold rent (escrow with DHCR or Housing Court)
1. DHCR determines rent reduction percentage

**Good Cause Eviction Challenge:**

1. Appear in Housing Court at eviction proceeding
1. Raise unreasonable rent increase as defense
1. Calculate local rent standard for applicable period
1. Provide evidence of rent increase amount & timing
1. Court determines if increase exceeds local standard; can dismiss eviction

## Policy Analysis Notes for Analysts

### Current Policy Issues & Trends

**1. Federal Funding Uncertainty (Section 8)**

- EHV sunset creates immediate pressure on vulnerable households
- HCV funding levels at political risk; potential future reductions
- Policy opportunity: Advocate for HCV funding preservation and expansion
- Risk: Future federal cuts could eliminate 85,000 vouchers in NYC

**2. Rent Stabilization Sustainability Questions**

- Rent increase caps (3-4.5%) vs. operating cost inflation
- Deferred maintenance concerns in older buildings
- Policy tension: Affordability vs. building viability
- Possible future reforms: Hardship provisions, targeted increases

**3. Good Cause Eviction Implementation**

- New protection for market-rate tenants (48% of rental market)
- Local rent standard compliance still emerging
- Litigation expected to clarify provisions
- Policy note: Bridges gap between regulated (44%) and unregulated (48%) tenants

**4. Rental Assistance Program Fragmentation**

- Multiple programs with different rules (Section 8, NYC 15/15, SCRIE, DRIE, ERA)
- Coordination challenges; application burden on tenants
- Policy opportunity: Unified application, simplified income verification

**5. Affordability Crisis Context**

- 352,000 NYC households in rental assistance programs
- Millions more ineligible but cost-burdened (>30% income on rent)
- Waitlist lag: Thousands waiting for Section 8 eligibility
- Policy gap: Unmet demand for affordable housing

### Key Policy Questions for Future Research

- What is the long-term fiscal impact of current rent increase caps on property tax revenue?
- How many Section 8 vouchers could be created with additional federal/state funding?
- What proportion of NYC renters lack access to any affordability program?
- How effective are current regulatory mechanisms in preventing displacement?
- What is optimal balance between rent control stringency and property investment incentives?
