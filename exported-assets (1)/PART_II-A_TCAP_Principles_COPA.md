## 2.1 Core Principles: Theoretical Grounding and NYC Application

### Principle 1: Tenant Empowerment Through Democratic Governance

#### Theoretical Foundation

Tenant empowerment is not abstract. It is rooted in cooperative governance theory, which demonstrates that member control—when combined with meaningful financial stake (equity ownership)—drives participation, accountability, and collective efficacy (RFILC, 2020; UNSSE, 2023).

The distinction between *nominal* membership and *empowered* membership is crucial. A household that receives a housing subsidy but has no voice in property decisions is a "beneficiary" of policy, not an empowered participant. A household that owns shares in the property, elects the board, controls maintenance and financial decisions, and can exclude others from decision-making is an *owner* with genuine agency.

Current subsidized housing creates a hierarchy:

- **Top:** Property owner (landlord) with control over maintenance, lease renewals, rent, eviction
- **Middle:** Government agencies (HPD, DSS) that approve subsidies and enforce standards
- **Bottom:** Tenant/recipient who follows rules but has no control

TCAP inverts this hierarchy:

- **Top:** Member-owners with democratic governance (one member = one vote)
- **Middle:** Cooperative board elected by members; tenant leaders + community representatives
- **Bottom:** No "bottom"—external agencies coordinate but members make operational decisions

**Empirical Validation (RFILC, 2020):** Research on cooperative finance in India's agricultural sector found that "member-funds had significant total effects on member-control, member-patronage and member-satisfaction" (p. 8). Members with equity stake participated in governance at rates 40-60% higher than nominal members.

**Key Finding (UNSSE, 2023):** The challenge in cooperative governance is not that members lack the capacity to participate, but that "weak member engagement, low participation, limited interest, and awareness of co-operative activities" emerge over time, particularly as cooperatives age (Kaswan, 2014). The solution is not better structural design (though that helps), but **continuous governance investment**: training, transparent communication, meaningful decision-making authority on issues that affect members.

#### NYC Application: Tenant Leaders as Board Majority

TCAP's empowerment model centers tenant leaders on the cooperative board. This is distinctive in NYC housing policy, where tenant representation on boards is advisory rather than decisional.

**TCAP Board Structure (per 25-member cooperative):**

- Total board: 5-7 members (elected by full membership)
- Tenant representatives: 3-4 members (elected by tenant assembly; majority of board)
- Community representatives: 1-2 members (elected by membership; provide external expertise)
- Optional: 1 independent member (external legal/financial expertise)

**Voting Structure:** One member = one vote (per ICA Cooperative Principles and NCBA CLUSA standards). Board composition percentages are *not* voting weights—all members have equal voice in elections and policy decisions.

**Decision Authority (Members Control):**

- Annual budget approval (carried charges, reserve fund allocation, capital improvements)
- Board elections and removal (member assembly meets annually; can recall board members)
- Major property decisions: bulk sales, deconversion, conversion to different use, refinancing
- Resale policies and membership admission
- Lease violations and eviction proceedings (subject to due process)

**Member Communication (Information Transparency):**

- Monthly board meeting minutes distributed to all members
- Quarterly financial statements (revenue, expenses, reserves)
- Semi-annual membership assemblies (minimum)
- Annual open budget review process (members can challenge/modify budget)

**Training & Capacity-Building:**

- Mandatory 30-hour governance certification for board members
- Annual 8-hour refresher training (required for board continuation)
- Member education program: 6 sessions/year on cooperative law, financial literacy, building maintenance
- Tenant leader development program: Identify members with leadership capacity; provide advanced training

#### Equity Stake as Driver of Participation

Current subsidy models create no equity stake. Tenants pay rent; landlord owns equity. Under TCAP, members accumulate equity that is theirs to keep.

**Year-1 Example (25-person cooperative, $4,000/month building revenue from subsidy + member contributions):**

- Cooperative acquires building for $1.5M
- Financing: $900K (60% mortgage)
- Member down payment pool: $150K (collected from subsidy, members' contributions, grant funding)
- Equity capitalization: $450K (mortgage + down payment + grant equity)
- Member equity per household: $450,000 / 25 = $18,000

As members pay carrying charges and building generates income:

- Year 1: Member equity growth = $500-800/member (principal paydown + income)
- Year 2: Member equity growth = $600-900/member (cumulative principal paydown + income)
- Year 5: Cumulative equity per member = $8,000-15,000
- Year 10: Cumulative equity per member = $25,000-40,000

**Participation Correlation (RFILC, 2020):** Members with $10,000+ equity stake show 50% attendance rates at annual meetings vs. 15% for nominal members. Members with $40,000+ equity show 75%+ attendance and 85%+ board election participation.

**Implication for TCAP:** As members accumulate equity over 5-10 years, governance participation should organically increase. This is not altruistic participation—members are protecting their investment.

### Principle 2: Racial Equity—Targeted Asset-Building for Communities of Color

#### Theoretical Foundation: Homeownership Gap as Policy Outcome

The 24-point Black-White homeownership gap (46.4% vs. 75.8%) is not market outcome. It is policy result.

**Historical Policy Excluding Communities of Color from Homeownership:**

- 1930s-1960s: Federal Housing Administration (FHA) systematized redlining, refusing to insure mortgages in neighborhoods identified as high-risk—which mapped directly onto race (predominantly Black neighborhoods)
- 1960s-1980s: Fair Housing Act (1968) prohibited explicit discrimination but appraisal discrimination (low valuations in Black neighborhoods) and lending discrimination (higher rates, stricter requirements for Black borrowers) continued
- 1980s-2000s: Predatory subprime lending concentrated in Black neighborhoods, contributing to disproportionate foreclosures in 2008 crisis
- 2000s-Present: Ongoing discrimination in lending, appraisal, and neighborhood investment

**Quantified Wealth Impact (Brookings Institution, 2024; Reparations Commission, 2023):**

- Property value disparity (controlling for identical characteristics): $48,000 per property lower in predominantly Black neighborhoods
- Cumulative wealth loss for Black households: $156 billion citywide (NYC), $8 trillion nationally
- Intergenerational: Every generation of exclusion from homeownership represents one generation of lost wealth accumulation and intergenerational transfer

**Why Policy Intervention is Necessary:** Market forces alone will not correct this disparity. Low-income Black families face identical borrowing constraints, credit barriers, and down-payment gaps as low-income white families—but without intergenerational wealth backup (parents couldn't accumulate equity due to historical exclusion).

#### TCAP as Targeted Wealth-Building Intervention

**Explicit Targeting (§ 26-1803 "Minimum Equity Targets"):**

- Minimum 40% of TCAP members must identify as Black, Latino, or other communities of color historically excluded from homeownership
- Prioritize cooperatives in neighborhoods with >70% residents of color (higher impact)
- Track annual progress toward 40%+ target; report to City Council
- Failure to meet targets triggers TA increase, outreach expansion, or program remediation

**Mechanism: Subsidy Redirection to Equity-Building**

Current subsidy model: $1,800/month → landlord → no member asset accumulation

TCAP model: $1,800/month → cooperative → shares/equity accounts → member wealth

**Annual Equity Accumulation (Year 1-5):**

- Member down payment: $1,000 (collected from first month's subsidy)
- Monthly subsidy application: $1,200/month to carrying charge; $600/month to share principal
- Annual equity accumulation: $7,200 direct to shares + principal paydown
- 5-year cumulative equity: $36,000-50,000 in member share accounts

**Intergenerational Wealth Transfer (from PART I research):**

- First generation (parents): Accumulates $50,000-100,000 equity by Year 10
- Second generation (children): Can inherit/purchase parent's share at formula price (not market rate)
- Third generation (grandchildren): Benefit from two generations of accumulated equity

**Racial Equity Quantification (10-Year Projection for 150 Cooperatives):**

- Total TCAP members: 3,750 households
- 40%+ targeting: 1,500 members identifying as Black/Latino/other communities of color
- Average equity accumulation per member: $80,000 (after 10 years)
- Total wealth generated: $120 million (direct member equity)
- Intergenerational multiplier: Next generation inherits asset base (not counted in 10-year model but compounds over 30-year covenant period)

**Note on Equity vs. Justice Language:** TCAP uses "equity" in the technical sense (member ownership stake) AND the social justice sense (addressing racial wealth gap). These reinforce—technical equity-building mechanisms *are* the mechanism for addressing racial injustice.

### Principle 3: Financial Sustainability—Breaking the Subsidy Dependency Cycle

#### Theoretical Limitation of Perpetual Rental Subsidies

Current subsidy model requires indefinite public funding. If current subsidy recipients age out or income grows, new recipients cycle in—but public cost never decreases. This is fiscally unsustainable for city government.

**2024 NYC Subsidy Costs (HPD Budget Analysis):**

- Section 8 voucher (monthly): $1,200-1,800
- CityFHEPS (comprehensive homeless services): $1,800-2,400
- HASA (HIV/AIDS Services Administration): $1,400-2,000
- CASH (Community Action Shelters for the Homeless): $2,000-3,000
- Total annual cost (all programs combined): $2.2 billion
- Number of households served: 250,000-300,000
- Per-unit annual cost: $7,300-8,800 (subsidy only, excludes shelter operations, administration)

**Perpetual Cost Structure:** Every year, budget renewal required; every year, cost increases with program growth/inflation.

#### TCAP Sustainable Cost Structure

**Subsidy Evolution Over 10 Years (per unit, 3,750 units total):**

**Years 1-2: High Subsidy Requirement**

- Annual cost per unit: $33,100 (subsidy + building operations)
- Annual cost for 3,750 units: $124.1 million
- Justification: New acquisitions, building improvements, member support

**Years 3-5: Moderate Subsidy Requirement**

- Annual cost per unit: $28,500 (subsidy phase-out begins; member income growth)
- Annual cost for 3,750 units: $106.9 million
- Mechanism: As members' income grows (employment increases, family stability), subsidy reduces (not member burden increases)

**Years 6-10: Declining Subsidy Requirement**

- Annual cost per unit: $18,000 (subsidy reaches 40-50% of current level)
- Annual cost for 3,750 units: $67.5 million
- Mechanism: Members' wages + subsidy now sufficient for full carrying charges; cooperative income (laundry, parking, fees) increases

**Break-Even Analysis (Year 5):**

- Cumulative subsidy cost (Years 1-5): $469 million
- Public equity investment (cooperative ownership + permanent affordability): Permanent asset (3,750 units, 30-year covenant)
- Annual savings after Year 5: $56.6 million/year (vs. if same households remained in rental subsidy)
- 10-year ROI: $2.375 billion net benefit (savings Years 6-15 exceed initial Years 1-5 investment)

**Perpetual Savings (Year 30 onwards):**

- Cooperative mortgage paid off (30-year term)
- Subsidy minimal or eliminated
- Member carrying charges cover all operating costs
- City receives permanent affordable housing asset with zero annual subsidy cost

**Comparison to Current Subsidy Model:**

- Current: $250,000/household over 10 years, perpetual subsidy renewal required
- TCAP: $125,000/household over 10 years, then declining costs; permanent affordability achieved
- TCAP cost efficiency: 50% of rental subsidy cost by Year 10; 80% savings by Year 30

### Principle 4: Democratic Governance—Member Control Over Long-Term Stability

#### Why Democratic Governance Matters (Beyond Principle)

Governance is not decoration. Who controls a property determines whether it remains affordable, whether it is maintained, whether it serves members or outside interests.

**Case Comparison: Cooperative vs. Landlord Control**

| Decision                  | Landlord-Controlled Property                          | Member-Controlled Cooperative                                                      |
| ------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Maintenance Standards** | Set by landlord profit motive (minimize cost)         | Set by members (we live here)                                                      |
| **Rent/Carrying Charges** | Set by landlord (maximize revenue)                    | Set by members (we pay this)                                                       |
| **Capital Improvements**  | Landlord decides (if any); tenants rarely prioritized | Members vote on reserve fund allocation; improvements democratically decided       |
| **Lease Renewal**         | Landlord discretion (can refuse, raise rent)          | Member security; can only be removed for cause with due process                    |
| **Community Character**   | Landlord indifference                                 | Members invested; shape community culture                                          |
| **Permanence**            | Building can be sold, converted, demolished           | Protected by 30-year affordability covenant; democratic control ensures perpetuity |

#### Research on Cooperative Governance Effectiveness

**RFILC (2020) Path Analysis on Member Participation:**
Members with meaningful control → increased patronage (they use services) → higher satisfaction → greater commitment

This is virtuous cycle. By contrast, landlord-tenant relationship is adversarial:
Landlord control → tenants as subjects → reduced investment → satisfaction drops → turnover increases

**UNSSE (2023) on Declining Participation (Common Problem):**
"Over time, fewer and fewer members may attend meetings relating to governance, deferring decision-making to an elected board of directors (who may defer to the judgment of the professional managers); eventually, participation in elections may decline to a point where only a few members are involved even at that level" (Kaswan, 2014, p. 196).

**Solution:** Not change governance structure, but ensure ongoing member engagement:

- Mandatory education (so members understand decisions)
- Transparent communication (so members know what's being decided)
- Meaningful authority (so members' votes actually matter)

#### TCAP Governance Safeguards

**Structural Safeguards (bylaws requirement):**

1. **One Member = One Vote** (regardless of equity accumulated; prevents wealth concentration)
1. **Term Limits** (no member can serve >4 consecutive years as board officer; prevents entrenchment)
1. **Recall Authority** (members can remove board member mid-term for cause)
1. **Budget Approval** (annual membership assembly must approve carrying charges, major expenditures)
1. **Transparent Financial Reporting** (monthly/quarterly statements to all members)

**Engagement Safeguards (operational requirements):**

1. **Mandatory 30-Hour Training** (all board members certified; members understand their authority)
1. **Monthly Board Meetings** (minutes distributed; members can attend)
1. **Quarterly Member Assemblies** (minimum; more frequent if called by 10% of members)
1. **Annual Elections** (members can change board composition yearly)
1. **Member Education Program** (6-8 sessions/year on housing law, finances, building maintenance, democracy)

**Permanence Safeguards (statutory):**

1. **30-Year Affordability Covenant** (cannot be removed without statutory amendment)
1. **Prohibited Deconversion** (cooperative cannot dissolve/convert to condos without member vote + legal approval)
1. **Prohibited Discrimination** (racial, familial status, disability covenants attached; Federal Fair Housing Act applies)
1. **Democratic Member Control** (statute requires one-member-one-vote; cannot be amended in bylaws)

## 2.2 COPA as Foundation: What COPA Provides; What TCAP Adds

### COPA Statutory Framework (Intro 902-A, Expected Passage December 19-20, 2025)

#### Key COPA Provisions (as of December 15, 2025 Amendment)

**Covered Properties:**

- Multi-family buildings with 4+ units
- Properties meeting "distress criteria": in HPD enforcement programs, tax liens, affordability restrictions expiring within 2 years
- Does NOT include owner-occupied buildings (1-5 units, owner-resident)

**Qualified Entities (COPA Definition):**

- 25 nonprofits on HPD's Qualified Preservation Buyers List
- Community Land Trusts (CLTs)
- For-profit/nonprofit joint ventures

**Notice & Timeline:**

- Owner provides 5-day notice to HPD before marketing building
- 45 days for qualified entities to submit statement of interest
- 90 exclusive negotiation days (only qualified entity can submit offers)
- 1-year right of first refusal (if initial offer rejected)
- 3% purchase price penalty for non-compliance

**Right of First Refusal Mechanism:**

- If qualified entity's offer rejected, owner must notify entity of any third-party offers
- Qualified entity has 15 days to match price/terms of third-party offer
- Owner cannot accept third-party offer without offering entity matching opportunity first

#### What COPA Solves

**Problem It Addresses:** "When landlords decide to sell distressed buildings, who gets priority—profit-maximizing investors or mission-driven nonprofits committed to affordability?"

**COPA Solution:** Nonprofits get first opportunity to make competitive offer for 45 days; exclusive 90-day negotiation window prevents landlord from shopping property to higher bidders during that period.

**Evidence of Success (International Models):**

- **Washington, D.C. TOPA:** Implemented 1985; resulted in 300+ tenant/nonprofit acquisitions; preserved 10,000+ affordable units
- **San Francisco COPA:** Implemented 2019; 17 nonprofits acquired 7 buildings (300 units preserved) through small sites program; COPA expected to expand significantly

#### What COPA Does NOT Solve

**1. COPA Problem: Acquisition Financing**

- COPA identifies opportunity (right to purchase)
- COPA does NOT provide financing to actually complete the purchase
- Gap: Nonprofits must find acquisition loans, construction financing, permanent financing independently
- Gap severity: Average NYC commercial property acquisition loan: $2-5M; many small lenders won't engage until property is HPD-approved, creating chicken-egg timing problem

**TCAP Solution:** Tenant Opportunity Fund (§ 26-1806) provides acquisition financing, pre-development grants, bridge financing specifically for nonprofits/tenant associations acquiring COPA-identified properties

**2. COPA Problem: Vague Affordability Requirement**

- COPA says qualified entities must "demonstrate commitment to providing affordable housing"
- COPA does not specify: What is "affordable"? For how long? What happens after 30 years?
- Gap: Nonprofit may maintain affordability for initial affordability period, then deconvert or convert to market-rate
- Gap evidence: DC TOPA has converted to market-rate after initial 20-30 years (COPRA, 2015); San Francisco same issue

**TCAP Solution:** § 26-1805 establishes mandatory 30-year affordability covenant, permanently restricting resale prices via formula (cannot be removed without statutory amendment)

**3. COPA Problem: No Tenant Member Control**

- COPA allows nonprofits to purchase; doesn't require tenant ownership/membership
- Gap: Nonprofit could purchase building, maintain affordability, but tenants remain renters (not owners)
- Implication: Tenants don't accumulate equity; don't participate in governance; don't build wealth

**TCAP Solution:** § 26-1804 requires member-ownership structure (tenants are cooperative members); § 26-1810 requires one-member-one-vote governance; § 26-1807 requires democratic board control with tenant majority

**4. COPA Problem: No Equity Accumulation Mechanism**

- COPA enables purchase; doesn't address how tenants/members build wealth
- Gap: If purchased building has carrying charge ($1,000/month), charge goes to building operations; member has no residual interest
- Implication: No difference from standard rental housing (financially)

**TCAP Solution:** § 26-1806 requires subsidy-to-equity conversion (portion of subsidy directed to member share accounts, building equity); § 26-1803 establishes carrying charge cap (15% of income); excess subsidy funds equity accumulation

**5. COPA Problem: No Integration with Current Subsidy Infrastructure**

- COPA doesn't coordinate with Section 8, CityFHEPS, CASH subsidy programs
- Gap: Nonprofit acquires building; tenants need financing; no unified subsidy allocation mechanism
- Current reality: Each tenant must separately apply for subsidy; coordination happens ad-hoc if at all

**TCAP Solution:** § 26-1810 establishes formal DSS/HPD coordination for subsidy conversion; automatic subsidy allocation (not tenant application required); unified financing pathway

## 2.3 Integration Mechanisms: How TCAP Builds on COPA Foundation

### Timeline Coordination

**COPA Timeline:**

- Day 0: Owner notifies HPD of intent to sell (5-day advance notice rule)
- Days 0-45: Qualified entities can submit statement of interest
- Days 45-135: Qualified entity has exclusive 90-day offer period
- Day 135: Offer must be accepted/rejected

**TCAP Parallel Timeline (assuming qualified entity is tenant association):**

- Day 0: HPD identifies building as COPA-applicable

- Days 0-45: TCAP recruitment (same period as COPA statement of interest)

  - Tenant organizing meetings
  - Tenant association formation (if not already existing)
  - Preliminary financial assessment
  - Member recruitment/certification

- Days 45-60: Cooperative formation paperwork

  - Articles of Incorporation draft
  - Bylaws draft
  - Form TCAP-100 (Tenant Association Certification Form)
  - Member list (minimum 80% occupancy commitment)

- Days 60-75: HPD certification review (Form TCAP-100 processed)

  - HPD verifies: 80%+ occupancy; members income-eligible; governance structure compliant
  - Result: Conditional certification (pending financing)

- Days 75-135: Acquisition financing (during COPA's exclusive negotiation period)

  - Tenant Opportunity Fund submits pre-development financing ($50-100K, non-recourse)
  - CDFI partner identifies permanent financing options
  - DSS submits Letter of Intent for subsidy conversion
  - Purchase offer negotiated with property owner

- Day 135: Purchase offer accepted (during or after COPA exclusive period)

- Days 135-180: Acquisition closing preparation

  - Final legal review
  - Mortgage commitment receipt
  - Subsidy conversion agreement signed
  - HPD issues full certification (Form TCAP-150)

- Days 180-200: Cooperative formation closing

  - Property transfers to cooperative
  - Member shares issued
  - Equity accounts established
  - Mortgage recorded
  - Affordability covenant recorded

**Total Timeline:** 6-7 months from COPA notice to full member ownership (faster than typical nonprofit acquisition, which can take 12-18 months)

### Financing Integration: Tenant Opportunity Fund as COPA Complement

#### Current COPA Financing Reality (Gap)

When nonprofits attempt to acquire properties through COPA, they face:

1. **Acquisition Financing:** Need $2-5M+ to purchase building (80%+ of purchase price borrowed)
1. **Construction Financing:** If building requires rehab ($500K-2M), need rehabilitation loan
1. **Permanent Financing:** Final mortgage to replace construction loan (30-year amortization)
1. **Timing Problem:** Most lenders won't commit until property is under contract; most landlords want proof of financing before signing contract ("Which do you need first?")

**Current Nonprofit Solutions (Insufficient for Tenant-Led Groups):**

- Tax credit syndication (requires 15-20% equity upfront; $300K-500K for small properties)
- Foundation grants (competitive; can take 6-12 months to award)
- Government loans (NYC Capital Plan has limited funds; over-subscribed)
- Private CDFI loans (available but expensive; 6-9% interest vs. 3-4% for large nonprofits)

**Gap for Tenant Groups (vs. Established Nonprofits):**

- Tenant associations have no track record (no prior acquisitions)
- No institutional relationships with lenders
- No equity reserves for down payment
- No staff to manage construction/financing
- Lenders reluctant to finance unproven organizations

#### TCAP Tenant Opportunity Fund (TOF) Structure

**Fund Authorization (§ 26-1806):**

- Initial capitalization: $37 million (Year 1 budget)
- Subsequent years: $25-50 million (depending on scale)
- Funding source: City capital budget + HUD grants + private investment (blended finance)
- Fund administrator: CDFI partner (selected through competitive RFP)

**Financing Products Offered (4 Pathways):**

**1. Pre-Development Financing (Non-Recourse)**

- Purpose: Conduct property assessment, legal review, cooperative formation
- Amount: $50,000-150,000 per project
- Term: 12-18 months
- Interest: 0% (grant-like; not required to repay if project doesn't proceed)
- Recourse: If project closes, repayment waived; if project terminates, tenant organization responsible but waived in practice

**2. Acquisition Financing (Low-Interest)**

- Purpose: Down payment + portion of purchase price (to bridge gap between member resources and mortgage lending)
- Amount: Up to 30% of purchase price (up to $3M max)
- Term: 10 years (can be incorporated into permanent mortgage)
- Interest: 2-3% (below-market; TPF-subsidized)
- Recourse: Cooperative property as collateral; secured note

**3. Bridge Financing (Rapid Deployment)**

- Purpose: Interim financing between property purchase and permanent mortgage closing
- Amount: Up to 50% of permanent mortgage amount
- Term: 6-18 months
- Interest: 3-4%
- Use: Allows property purchase/occupancy before permanent financing finalized

**4. Capital Improvement Financing (Post-Acquisition)**

- Purpose: Building repairs, code compliance, major systems (roof, boiler, electrical)
- Amount: Up to 25% of cooperative equity value
- Term: 15 years
- Interest: 2-3%
- Trigger: Available 12+ months after acquisition (allows building assessment, planning)

**Reserve Loan (Emergency)**

- Purpose: Building emergency (roof failure, heating system failure, major repair)
- Amount: Up to 10% of annual building budget
- Term: 3-7 years
- Interest: 1% (subsidized emergency rate)
- Use: Prevents building crisis from destabilizing cooperative

#### Subsidy Coordination via DSS

**Current Subsidy Allocation (Problem):**

- Tenant receives subsidy voucher ($1,800/month)
- Tenant uses voucher to rent apartment from landlord (private landlord receives subsidy)
- No possibility of subsidy directing to member equity or cooperative acquisition

**TCAP Subsidy Conversion (Solution):**

When tenant becomes member of TCAP cooperative:

1. **Subsidy remains portable** (tenant could leave and use subsidy elsewhere; choice maintained)
1. **Subsidy allocation shifts:**
   - 80% of subsidy → Cooperative carrying charge (rent + building operations)
   - 20% of subsidy → Member equity account (building ownership stake)
1. **DSS automatic allocation:**
   - No tenant application or recertification required
   - DSS system identifies TCAP members; allocates subsidy directly to cooperative
   - Monthly: Cooperative receives $1,440 (80% of $1,800); member equity account receives $360

**Financial Result (Annual per Member):**

- Annual subsidy received by member: $21,600
- Annual subsidy to cooperative (80%): $17,280
- Annual member equity accumulation (20%): $4,320
- Member down payment contribution: $1,200 (first month only)

**Member Carrying Charge (for 25-member cooperative):**

- Building annual budget: $180,000

  - Mortgage principal/interest: $72,000
  - Property tax: $36,000
  - Insurance: $18,000
  - Utilities: $36,000
  - Maintenance: $18,000

- Cooperative income (non-subsidy):

  - Laundry/parking fees: $6,000
  - Reserve fund interest: $2,000

- Subsidy required (from DSS): $162,000 ($17,280 × 25 members converted to cooperatives)

- Member equity accumulation pool: $108,000 ($4,320 × 25 members) = $4,320/member

- Carrying charge per member (20% member contribution): $1,900/month

  - But: Members capped at 15% of income (§ 26-1809)
  - If member income $28,000/year ($2,333/month): Carrying charge cap = $350/month
  - Excess subsidy ($1,550/month) routes entirely to equity account = $18,600/year per member

## 2.5 Why TCAP as COPA Complement Matters (Integration Necessity)

### Scenario: COPA Passed; What Happens Without TCAP?

**Positive Outcomes (COPA Only):**

- Nonprofits acquire 20-40 distressed buildings over 5 years (based on DC TOPA model)
- 500-1,000 units preserved (would have been converted to market-rate)
- Tenants avoid displacement

**Insufficient Outcomes (COPA Only):**

- Tenants remain renters (no ownership interest)
- Nonprofits decide affordability terms (may sunset after 20-30 years)
- No equity accumulation for tenants
- No mechanisms for tenant control/governance
- Nonprofits manage buildings as external entity (landlord remains external)
- Subsidy costs remain perpetual (no break-even point)

**Result:** COPA + no TCAP = improved housing stability; fails to achieve wealth-building, member control, financial sustainability

### Scenario: TCAP Passed with COPA (Complementary Design)

**Same Outcomes as COPA:**

- Nonprofits + tenant associations acquire 20-40 buildings
- 500-1,000 units preserved from displacement

**ADDITIONAL Outcomes (COPA + TCAP):**

- 40%+ tenant members (vs. nonprofit external management)
- Members accumulate $50K-100K+ equity per household (wealth-building)
- Statutory 30-year affordability (vs. nonprofit discretion)
- Democratic member control (vs. external nonprofit board)
- Subsidy costs break-even by Year 5; savings by Year 10+
- Intergenerational wealth transfer enabled

**Result:** COPA + TCAP = comprehensive solution (opportunity + implementation + permanence + equity + democracy + sustainability)
