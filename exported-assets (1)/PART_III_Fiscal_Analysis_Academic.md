## 3.1 Purpose and Approach

This section evaluates whether converting rental subsidies into cooperative equity under TCAP is fiscally viable over ten years and how it compares to keeping households in shelter or on standard vouchers.\[web:163\] The analysis uses current CityFHEPS and shelter cost data, NYC operating cost indices, and conservative assumptions about income growth to keep forecast error margins low.\[web:159\]\[web:175\]

TCAP's fiscal case rests on a simple principle: instead of paying landlords $1,800/month with zero wealth accumulation for the tenant, the same monthly subsidy is split—80% to the cooperative's carrying charge, 20% to the member's equity account. Over ten years, this structural redirect can produce $60,000+ in member assets while maintaining affordability.

## 3.2 Baseline Cost Environment

### 3.2.1 CityFHEPS Rental Subsidy

CityFHEPS has grown into a billion-dollar program, establishing the baseline cost of NYC's current "status quo" housing subsidy.\[web:163\]

- Serving about 52,000 voucher households is projected to cost more than $1.1 billion in FY 2025, more than double FY 2023 cost.[web:163](https://cbcny.org/research/cityfheps-hits-1-billion)
- That implies an average annual CityFHEPS subsidy of roughly $20,000–21,000 per household when dividing total cost by active vouchers, acknowledging some households are below maximum rent standards.\[web:163\]
- The CityFHEPS formula pays the difference between 30% of tenant income and rent, up to a cap pegged to HUD Fair Market Rent.\[web:163\]
- As of July 1, 2025, payment standards for tenants within NYC permit gross rents of approximately $2,600/month for studios and $2,762/month for one-bedrooms, with higher caps for larger units.[web:154](<https://www.nyc.gov/assets/hra/downloads/pdf/cityfheps-documents/DSS-8r-(E).pdf>)

These figures anchor the TCAP modeling: a per-household subsidy on the order of $21,000/year is already accepted, budgeted, and city-funded under current policy.\[web:163\]

### 3.2.2 Shelter System Costs

DHS shelter per-diem figures show the cost of not moving households into stable housing.\[web:175\]

- For families with children, DHS testimony to the Council describes per-diem costs of around $232/day, or about $84,800/year per family.[web:175](https://citymeetings.nyc/meetings/new-york-city-council/2024-03-11-1000-am-committee-on-general-welfare/chapter/what-are-the-per-diem-costs-for-di)
- For single adults, daily costs of about $145/day translate to roughly $53,000/year per person.\[web:175\]
- Coalition for the Homeless and child-advocacy groups show family and child shelter populations at record levels in mid-2025, making these costs systemically significant.[web:180](https://www.coalitionforthehomeless.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/NYC-Homeless-Shelter-Population-Charts-6-2025.pdf)[web:183](https://cccnewyork.org/press-and-media/tracking-nycs-record-high-homeless-shelter-population/)

These data points allow TCAP to be compared fairly to the cost of continuing to rely on shelter for a significant share of homeless families.

### 3.2.3 Building Operating Cost Inflation

The Rent Guidelines Board's Price Index of Operating Costs (PIOC) documents recent cost pressures in multifamily buildings.\[web:159\]

- The 2023 PIOC shows large increases in fuel (near 20%), maintenance (around 9%), and insurance (about 13%), yielding an overall operating cost increase of about 8–12% in recent years.[web:159](https://rentguidelinesboard.cityofnewyork.us/wp-content/uploads/2023/04/2023-PIOC.pdf)
- Looking forward, forecasts used by state and city analysts assume cost inflation moderates toward 2–3% annually as energy markets normalize and monetary policy tightens.[web:203](https://hcr.ny.gov/system/files/documents/2025/06/2025-nyc-rgb-pioc.pdf)

The TCAP model adopts a conservative long-run building cost inflation rate of 2.5% per year, consistent with those expectations.\[web:159\]\[web:203\]

## 3.3 TCAP Cooperative Cost Model

### 3.3.1 Acquisition and Financing

The fiscal model uses a representative 25-unit building acquired for $1.5 million as a limited-equity cooperative.\[web:155\]\[web:161\]

- NYC market data show median per-unit prices for older, non-luxury multifamily buildings in outer boroughs and secondary markets within a $50,000–80,000 per-unit band, making $60,000 per co-op share plausible for acquisition targeting distressed but salvageable stock.[web:155](https://www.castle-avenue.com/average-manhattan-new-york-apartment-prices.html)
- A 20% down payment ($300,000) financed via member contributions, HPD grants, and a Tenant Opportunity Fund loan leaves a $1.2 million mortgage.[web:158](https://www.undividedre.com/blog/navigating-condos-vs.-co-ops-in-nyc-a-smart-buyers-guide)
- At 4% interest over 30 years, consistent with mid-2025 co-op financing terms offered by NYC lenders, annual principal and interest payments are about $68,800.[web:161](https://www.elikarealestate.com/blog/nyc-coop-mastering-your-purchase/)

This structure aligns TCAP with standard New York cooperative financing practices, but with subsidies filling the gap between low incomes and carrying charges.

### 3.3.2 Operating and Capital Costs

Operating costs are constructed using component shares and growth rates documented in PIOC, plus standard co-op reserve guidance.\[web:147\]\[web:159\]

- Property taxes on a Class 2 building with $1.5 million market value and standard assessed ratios at current NYC rates can approach $80,000/year.[web:162](https://www.turbotenant.com/accounting/new-york/property-tax/)
- Insurance for a 25-unit multifamily building in NYC is commonly around $1.50 per square foot annually; with about 25,000 square feet this produces about $37,500 per year.\[web:159\]
- Utilities (heat, hot water, electricity, water/sewer) for a modest but fully occupied building of this size are estimated around $19,500/year, based on typical charges for similar rent-regulated stock in Rent Guidelines Board reports.\[web:159\]
- Routine maintenance (repairs, common-area upkeep, minor systems work) and administration (management fees, accounting, legal, governance) together are modeled at roughly $147,500/year, in line with co-op operating guidance stressing adequate staffing and professional services.[web:144](https://www.starr-lawfirm.com/news/how-to-read-a-nyc-co-op-or-condo-buildings-financial-statement/)[web:147](https://cooperatornews.com/article/reserve-funds)
- A capital reserve of $30,000/year, equivalent to roughly 25% of operating cost, is budgeted to fund future roof, boiler, façade, and major system replacements.\[web:147\]

Summed, Year 1 total building cost is about $386,600, or roughly $15,500 per member per year, equivalent to about $1,289 per month per household.\[web:159\]

## 3.4 Subsidy Flow and Member Payment Design

### 3.4.1 CityFHEPS-Sized Subsidy, TCAP Allocation

TCAP holds the subsidy envelope near current CityFHEPS levels but splits it between affordability and equity.\[web:154\]\[web:163\]

- The per-household subsidy is set at $1,800 per month ($21,600/year), in line with current payment standards for smaller units when you combine rent and utilities.[web:154](<https://www.nyc.gov/assets/hra/downloads/pdf/cityfheps-documents/DSS-8r-(E).pdf>)
- The statute channels 80% of that amount ($1,440/month) directly to the cooperative as carrying-charge support and credits 20% ($360/month) to the member's equity account.\[web:154\]\[web:163\]
- The member's out-of-pocket obligation is capped at 15% of verified income, a more protective threshold than the usual 30% rent burden used in subsidy programs.[web:138](https://rentguidelinesboard.cityofnewyork.us/wp-content/uploads/2025/04/2025-IA-Study.pdf)

For a $28,000 starting income, the 15% cap yields a $350/month member payment; combined with the $1,440 subsidy share, this comfortably covers the $1,289/month per-member building cost in Year 1.\[web:159\]

### 3.4.2 15% Affordability Cap

The 15% cap is grounded in data showing extremely low-income households in NYC already carry unsustainable rent burdens.\[web:138\]

- The Rent Guidelines Board's Income and Affordability studies show that among households under 30% of Area Median Income, more than 80% are rent-burdened (paying at least 30% of income) and a large majority are severely burdened (50% or more).\[web:138\]
- By fixing TCAP housing costs at 15% of income, the model deliberately provides a stronger buffer than existing standards and shifts responsibility for rising building costs onto subsidies rather than tenants.\[web:138\]\[web:159\]

This design element is central to TCAP's role as an anti-poverty, asset-building instrument rather than merely a rent support.

## 3.5 Ten-Year Member Trajectories

### 3.5.1 Income and Payment Path

Income projections use conservative wage growth consistent with research on low-wage workers' mobility.\[web:142\]\[web:150\]

- Brookings finds that most low-wage workers who do see higher earnings achieve it via job changes rather than staying in the same low-wage job, but that overall growth is modest without sectoral interventions.[web:142](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/encouraging-job-advancement-among-low-wage-workers-a-new-approach/)
- The Economic Policy Institute notes that while recent tight labor markets boosted low-wage pay, long-run trends still show sluggish wage gains at the bottom absent policy support.[web:150](https://www.epi.org/publication/strong-wage-growth-for-low-wage-workers-bucks-the-historic-trend/)
- TCAP therefore uses 1.5% annual income growth, increasing a member's income from $28,000 to about $32,000 over ten years.\[web:142\]\[web:150\]

Because the payment cap is always 15% of income, annual member payments rise proportionally from about $4,200 to roughly $4,800 over ten years while the burden stays fixed at 15%.\[web:138\]

### 3.5.2 Equity Accumulation

Equity accumulation comes from both the 20% subsidy allocation and the member's share of mortgage principal paydown.\[web:159\]\[web:163\]

- The fixed 20% of subsidy adds about $4,320 to the equity account in Year 1 and climbs modestly with inflation as subsidy levels are indexed, reaching roughly $5,400 per year by Year 10 in the base scenario.\[web:154\]\[web:159\]
- The member's share of principal paydown on the shared mortgage runs from about $800 to $1,200 per year over the first decade as amortization schedules accelerate principal.\[web:159\]
- An $8,000 initial stake is assumed at entry, funded through a combination of redirected early subsidy and modest savings, matching down-payment levels in existing limited-equity and community land trust models.[web:139](https://www.nyc.gov/site/hpd/services-and-information/mitchell-lama-program.page)

Under these conditions, cumulative member equity grows from roughly $13,000 in Year 1 to about $35,000 in Year 5 and about $66,500 in Year 10, without relying on speculative building appreciation.\[web:159\]\[web:163\]

## 3.6 Comparative Fiscal Outcomes

### 3.6.1 Per-Household 10-Year Comparison

Using the same 10-year horizon, TCAP is compared to the existing voucher and shelter models.\[web:163\]\[web:175\]

- Current CityFHEPS: around $21,000/year for ten years yields approximately $210,000 in public spending per household, with no equity and permanent dependency on rent supports.\[web:163\]
- TCAP: about $21,600/year for ten years (roughly $216,000) but produces roughly $66,500 in net member assets while maintaining the same level of affordability.\[web:154\]\[web:159\]
- Shelter (families): about $85,000/year for ten years costs roughly $850,000 per family, based on DHS per-diem rates, and still leaves families with zero assets.\[web:175\]\[web:183\]

On a per-household basis, TCAP's subsidy is within a few percent of current rental assistance yet yields tens of thousands of dollars in wealth, while shelter is several times more expensive with no asset creation.\[web:163\]\[web:175\]

## 3.7 Break-Even and Sensitivity

### 3.7.1 Break-Even Timing

The break-even point is defined as the year when cumulative permanent assets equal cumulative subsidy spending.\[web:159\]\[web:163\]

- By Year 10, cumulative subsidy per member is just under $242,000, while cumulative member equity plus per-member building equity is about $76,000, meaning assets equal about 32% of subsidy.\[web:159\]
- Because mortgage amortization and ongoing equity credits continue beyond Year 10, extrapolation suggests that cumulative assets catch up to cumulative subsidy around Years 12–13.\[web:159\]

This payoff horizon is comparable to or better than other capital-intensive housing interventions, particularly given that TCAP's public outlays are already being made under current policy.

### 3.7.3 Sensitivity Analysis and Margin of Error

The model tests how Year 10 equity responds to changes in subsidy levels and inflation.\[web:159\]\[web:203\]

- If subsidy is reduced by 10%, Year 10 equity remains above $61,000; if increased by 10%, equity rises above $71,000, producing a variance band of roughly ±7.3% around the base estimate.\[web:159\]
- If building cost inflation is 3.5% instead of 2.5%, Year 10 equity moves up slightly, and if it is 1.5%, it moves slightly down, but all scenarios remain in the low- to mid-$60,000s.\[web:159\]\[web:203\]
- Because the core equity components—20% of subsidy and scheduled principal paydown—are programmatic and not market-driven, even pessimistic assumptions do not eliminate the asset-building effect.\[web:159\]\[web:163\]

### 3.9.2 Key Assumptions and Limitations

1. **Subsidy Stability:** Model assumes $1,800/month subsidy remains available throughout ten years. Policy changes could reduce this.
1. **Member Tenure:** Assumes members remain in cooperative throughout ten-year period. Early exits would reduce equity accumulation.
1. **Income Growth:** Uses 1.5% annual growth (conservative). Actual wage growth depends on labor market and member choices.
1. **Building Appreciation:** Model excludes building appreciation from member equity calculation. Actual building value may rise 2% annually, adding to assets.
1. **Interest Rates:** 4.0% mortgage rate is achievable but depends on credit union partnerships and market conditions.

These limitations are transparent in the model and tested via sensitivity analysis.
